Module 02 · R&D Discovery · Five-Day Theoretical Roadmap

The Perfumer's Bench.

Every medicine that ever reached a patient began as an invisible conversation between a protein and a molecule. This is how that conversation is engineered — from the brief, to the accord, to the bottle.

The hook

Out of roughly 10,000 promising molecules that enter a discovery program, only one survives to become a marketed drug. The discipline of R&D Discovery is not about chemistry — it is about knowing which 9,999 to kill, and when.

The elevator pitch

Discovery is the art of binding possibility to molecule.

Discovery is the upstream half of pharma R&D — the years spent in laboratories and computers before a compound ever touches a human. Four moves run in parallel: decide what in the body to attack (target), find molecules that attack it (hit-to-lead), choose a delivery form (modality), and let computation amplify all three (AI-driven discovery). Every decision is a bet against biology, and biology nearly always wins.

The master analogy — the perfumer's atelier

A master perfumer receives a brief: create a scent for a specific moment, mood, or person. In front of them sits an "organ" of hundreds of raw materials — essential oils, synthetic aroma molecules, animalic notes. They compose a formula, place a drop on a blotter, smell it, revise, and iterate — sometimes for years — until the composition obeys the brief and survives on human skin.

R&D Discovery is the same craft, at molecular scale. A disease biology defines the brief. A compound library is the perfumer's organ. A medicinal chemist is the nose. ADMET properties decide whether the composition survives the body's metabolism the way a perfume must survive skin chemistry. Modern AI simply gives the perfumer a new instrument — not a replacement for the nose, but a telescope over the organ.

Origin story

Why discovery exists at all.

Until the late 19th century, medicines were found, not designed — foxglove for the heart, willow bark for fever, opium for pain. Paul Ehrlich's 1909 discovery of arsphenamine (Salvarsan) for syphilis marked the first designed drug: he systematically screened 605 arsenical compounds against the spirochete. He called his approach the magische Kugel — the magic bullet — a single molecule to strike a single pathological target, sparing everything else.

That phrase still governs discovery a century later. It is why we speak of targets and selectivity, why we obsess over off-target effects, and why every generation of tools — from the early 20th century's animal pharmacology, to the 1980s' recombinant DNA, to the 2020s' protein language models — has chased the same question: can we design a molecule to hit exactly what we mean, and only what we mean?

Modern discovery still fails 90%+ of the time. Biology is inconvenient. But the fraction of designed-vs-found medicines grows every decade, and in the last five years, a new layer — generative AI over chemistry and biology — has begun to compress the timeline Ehrlich once measured in years into weeks.

Day 01 · The Landscape & The Target

Where discovery sits, and what we hunt for.

01
Bloom · Remember + Understand · ~2.5 hrs

Reading the brief — what makes a target a target

+

In 1996 Pfizer filed an IND for a compound called UK-92,480. It was meant to treat angina. It failed that trial. But patients in the trial refused to return their unused tablets. The compound became Viagra. Moral: targets can be wrong, and biology is louder than hypothesis.

Day 1Learning objectives

  • Place R&D Discovery on the full pharma pipeline map (Discovery → Preclinical → IND → Phase I-III → Approval)
  • Name the four sub-disciplines and what each produces as output
  • Understand target classes: GPCRs, kinases, nuclear receptors, ion channels, protein-protein interactions, enzymes
  • Grasp the evidence hierarchy: genetic > mechanistic > clinical correlative
  • Define druggability and explain why ~80% of the human proteome is "undruggable" by small molecules

The four key concepts

The target product profile (TPP)

A forward-looking one-page brief. Defines the patient population, dosing route, competitor comparisons, and efficacy/safety thresholds the eventual molecule must clear.

The perfumer's brief — "a woody unisex eau de parfum, 8h longevity, for a $180 retail tier" — before a single drop is composed.

Target classes — the ingredient families

GPCRs (~34% of approved drugs), kinases (~20%), nuclear receptors, ion channels, proteases. Each class has a characteristic binding pocket geometry that determines which chemical approaches work.

Florals, woods, musks, aldehydes, ambers. Each ingredient family demands different composition technique.

The evidence hierarchy

Genetic evidence (GWAS, rare variants, Mendelian disease) > mechanistic (CRISPR knockout, biochemistry) > clinical correlative (biomarker-disease association). Targets with human genetic validation roughly double the probability of regulatory success.

A scent backed by consumer sensory panels beats one backed only by the perfumer's preference.

Druggability

Not every target is attackable. A protein needs a well-defined binding pocket of the right geometry, polarity, and solvent accessibility. "Undruggable" targets — flat protein surfaces, transcription factors, intrinsically disordered regions — are the frontier.

Some molecules refuse to be dissolved in ethanol — their structure forbids a wearable perfume.

Most newcomers assume discovery begins at the bench. It does not. It begins with an argument about biology — a hypothesis that if you perturb protein X, disease Y improves. The best discovery scientists are first and foremost disease biologists. Molecules come later.

In your own words, explain why a GPCR is considered "more druggable" than a transcription factor — to someone who has never heard either term.

One articulation

A GPCR is a protein shaped like a small mailbox sticking out of a cell's surface, with a clear opening a drug molecule can slip into. A transcription factor floats inside the cell nucleus, shaped more like a sheet pressed against DNA — there is no obvious pocket for a molecule to wedge itself into. Historically, pockets are what small molecules can grip. No pocket, no grip, no drug.

G-P-C-R: Good Pockets, Clear Receptor. Small molecules live and die by whether a pocket exists.
T-P-P: Target the Patient & Price — the TPP frames both who you serve and what you must beat.
02
Bloom · Understand + Apply · ~3 hrs

From screen to lead — shaping the binder

+

A medicinal chemist once described their work as "sculpting a key for a lock you've never seen, using only echoes from a thousand nearly-fitting keys." That is hit-to-lead in a sentence.

Day 2Learning objectives

  • Compare four ways to find initial hits: HTS, fragment-based (FBDD), DNA-encoded libraries (DEL), virtual screening
  • Apply Lipinski's Rule of Five and explain why it exists
  • Decode ADMET (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, Toxicity) as a survival filter
  • Understand multi-parameter optimization (MPO) as a Pareto-front problem, not a ranking
  • Trace the DMTA (Design–Make–Test–Analyze) loop and why it is the central iteration unit of discovery

The four key concepts

Hit-finding — four doors into the library

HTS: test ~10⁶ compounds robotically against the target. FBDD: screen small fragments and grow them. DEL: tag each molecule with DNA barcode and screen 10⁹–10¹² in a tube. Virtual screening: dock compounds computationally before touching a flask.

Four ways a perfumer auditions raw materials: smell every bottle (HTS), test base notes first (FBDD), run all at once with sensor tags (DEL), or predict harmony before blending (virtual).

Lipinski's Rule of Five

A 1997 empirical rule — for oral small-molecule drugs: molecular weight ≤500, logP ≤5, H-bond donors ≤5, acceptors ≤10. Violating more than one predicts poor oral bioavailability. It is not physics; it is a survival statistic drawn from drugs that actually worked.

The wearability check — does this perfume survive on skin, in clothing, through a day? Too heavy, it suffocates. Too light, it vanishes.

ADMET — the body's obstacle course

A molecule that binds perfectly in a test tube must still be absorbed through the gut, distributed to the right tissue, survive metabolism (liver enzymes like CYP3A4), be excreted safely, and not trigger toxicity. Most hits die here, not at the target.

A stunning composition on the blotter can collapse on skin — the oils oxidise, the volatiles flee, the base clashes with body heat. ADMET is perfumery's "skin chemistry" at molecular level.

MPO & the DMTA loop

Optimization is multi-dimensional: you cannot maximize potency alone. MPO defines a desirability score across 5–10 properties, and the DMTA loop — Design a new analogue, Make it, Test it, Analyze results — is the weekly (or now daily) iteration engine of modern lead optimization.

Each blotter iteration: add 0.1% more iso-E-super, swap sandalwood source, re-smell, re-score. A single accord may see 40 iterations over 6 months.
Figure 2 · Multi-parameter optimization — the lead's radar profile
Mentor's note

A common mistake: optimizing potency until everything else breaks. The molecule that binds the target at 10 picomolar but also inhibits seventy other kinases will kill its patient before it treats them. Selectivity is not a luxury — it is the difference between a drug and a poison with paperwork.

Day 2 checkpoint

  • Explain the difference between potency and efficacy
  • Recite the four ADMET failure modes in order of a molecule's journey through the body
  • Describe the DMTA loop and its typical cycle time (weeks → days with automation)
  • Explain why MPO is a Pareto problem, not a ranked list
Feynman teach-back

A colleague says "our compound has IC50 of 2 nM, we're done." What three follow-up questions should you ask before you believe them?

click to reveal →
Three questions

One: against what other targets did you profile it — selectivity? Two: what is its metabolic stability in human liver microsomes — will it survive first-pass? Three: what is the free fraction — the unbound drug that actually reaches the target inside cells, not the total drug in plasma? Binding in a test tube tells you almost nothing about binding in a patient.

Memory hook
A-D-M-E-T: Absorb, Deliver, Metabolize, Excrete, Tolerate — the body's five-gate obstacle course every molecule must pass. Forget one gate and the drug dies there.
D-M-T-A: Dream, Make, Measure, Adjust — the weekly rhythm of lead optimization.
03
Bloom · Apply + Analyze · ~3 hrs

The modality zoo — choosing the vehicle

+

In 2014 you could not have treated severe eczema without stepping down to immunosuppressants with miserable side effects. In 2017 dupilumab arrived — a bispecific antibody against IL-4Rα. The target (Th2 cytokine signaling) had been known for two decades. The modality — a fully human monoclonal — is what changed. Modality choice is a strategic lever, not a technical detail.

Day 3Learning objectives

  • Map the modality zoo: small molecule, monoclonal antibody, ADC, bispecific, CAR-T/TCR-T, gene therapy (AAV), mRNA, oligonucleotides (ASO/siRNA), radioligand, PROTAC, molecular glue
  • Connect each modality to its signature delivery technology (LNP, AAV capsid, conjugation chemistry)
  • Compare modalities across six axes: size, specificity, manufacturability, delivery complexity, durability, cost
  • Distinguish platform value from asset value and name three reusable platforms
  • Recognize emerging modalities: base editing, prime editing, in-vivo CAR, circular RNA, radiotheranostics

The four key concepts

Small vs. large — the size axis

Small molecules (≤500 Da) slip into intracellular pockets but can hit off-targets; biologics (mAbs, ~150 kDa) are exquisitely specific but cannot reach inside cells. Size determines where in the body's architecture the molecule can operate.

A citrus top-note evaporates in minutes; a musk base lingers for hours. Volatility and persistence map almost directly to small molecule vs. biologic pharmacokinetics.

Delivery — the vehicle problem

Most novel modalities fail not on the payload but on delivery. mRNA needs LNPs (lipid nanoparticles); gene therapy needs AAV capsids; ADCs need stable but cleavable linkers; siRNA needs GalNAc conjugation for liver targeting. Delivery science is its own discipline.

The most exquisite ingredient is useless if you cannot get it onto the skin. Fixatives, solvents, aerosol design — perfume delivery science.

Platform vs. asset value

An asset is one molecule for one disease. A platform is a reusable engine that produces many assets — Moderna's mRNA platform, Seagen's ADC linker chemistry, Alnylam's GalNAc-siRNA. Platforms demand higher upfront investment but compound returns across indications.

A signature base accord (oakmoss, labdanum, civet) the perfumer reuses across a whole fragrance wardrobe — each new launch built faster than the last.

Developability — will this be manufacturable?

A beautiful molecule that cannot be manufactured at scale is a failure. Biologics face aggregation, stability, immunogenicity, and CMC costs. Developability scoring (stability + expression yield + aggregation propensity + immunogenicity) decides which clones advance — often before any animal work.

If the formula uses an ingredient banned in Europe or costing €800/kg, it will never reach market. Developability is the factory's veto.
Figure 3 · The modality matrix — size vs. delivery complexity
Mentor's note

Modality choice is where biology meets business. A small molecule may address the same target as a biologic but at one-tenth the cost of goods and twice the patient convenience. A gene therapy may require a one-time $2M infusion. The right modality is rarely the most scientifically elegant — it is the one the reimbursement system, manufacturing network, and patient adherence profile can all tolerate simultaneously.

  • Explain why an ADC is a "biologic with a small-molecule payload"
  • Name three reusable platforms and the companies that built them
  • Articulate the developability tradeoffs for a bispecific antibody
Feynman teach-back

Your team found a disease-causing transcription factor inside nuclear pores. A colleague suggests a small-molecule inhibitor. What modality alternatives would you offer, and why?

click to reveal →
One articulation

Transcription factors typically lack deep binding pockets, so small molecules struggle. Alternatives: PROTACs that tag the transcription factor for cellular degradation (no pocket needed, just a binder); molecular glues that bring it into proximity with an E3 ligase; antisense oligonucleotides or siRNA to silence its mRNA; or if the patient population is small and durable effect is needed, gene therapy to edit the underlying genetic cause. Each buys a different risk/reward.

Memory hook
S-O-B-C-A: Small, Oligo, Bio, Conjugate, Advanced. The five modality tiers, small to large. Progress through the ladder trades accessibility for precision, and cost for durability.
04
Bloom · Analyze + Evaluate · ~3.5 hrs

The computational revolution — AI as the new nose

+

In July 2021, DeepMind released AlphaFold2, which predicted protein structures with near-experimental accuracy. In August 2022 the full human proteome — 200 million structures — was free on the internet. A century of experimental crystallography, replicated in a GPU-afternoon. The question for discovery is no longer can we know the structure? It is now that we know, how do we design?

Day 4Learning objectives

  • Decode molecular representations: SMILES, graph neural networks, molecular fingerprints, 3D conformers
  • Separate what AlphaFold does (static structure prediction) from what it does not (dynamics, binding affinity, multimeric conformations)
  • Differentiate discriminative models (QSAR, property prediction) from generative models (diffusion, RL, VAEs)
  • Understand protein language models (ESM, ProGen) as the biological analogue of LLMs
  • Frame free-energy perturbation (FEP) as the physics-based gold standard for binding affinity prediction

The four key concepts

Molecular representation — how computers see chemistry

SMILES: a text encoding of a molecule (e.g. CC(=O)Oc1ccccc1C(=O)O for aspirin). Graphs: atoms as nodes, bonds as edges — fed into graph neural networks. Fingerprints: fixed-length bit vectors of structural substructures. 3D conformers: spatial coordinates, needed for structure-based work.

Four ways to describe a scent to a machine: its ingredient list (SMILES), the connections between notes (graph), its sensory fingerprint (floral-woody-amber bits), or the literal molecule shape (3D).

AlphaFold & its limits

AlphaFold predicts the static folded structure of a single protein chain from its amino acid sequence, with remarkable accuracy for proteins similar to known structures. It is not reliable for: protein dynamics, binding affinity, orphan or intrinsically disordered proteins, or co-folded complexes with unusual partners. AlphaFold3 extends to some ligands and complexes — partially.

It shows you the finished dress on a mannequin — not how it moves on a dancer, not how it fits a different body.

Generative chemistry

Discriminative models score existing molecules. Generative models propose new ones under constraints. Modern approaches: RNNs on SMILES (e.g. REINVENT), graph-VAEs, and diffusion models (Chemformer, Evotec's RoseTTAFold variants). Output quality depends on the training data and reward signal.

Instead of tasting 1,000 perfumes to find one you like, you describe what you want and the system proposes 50 candidate formulas you have never smelled.

Protein language models

ESM (Evolutionary Scale Modeling) and ProGen treat amino acid sequences like sentences, learning from tens of millions of protein sequences. They can predict function, stability, variant effects, and — with diffusion partners like RFdiffusion — design entirely new proteins from scratch (de novo protein design).

A language model that learned to write new poems in a style it was trained on — except the language is proteins and the "poems" are functional enzymes.

Mentor's note

The greatest risk of this era is AI-theater. A team deploys an impressive-sounding foundation model, publishes a blog post, and produces zero leads. The right frame: AI in discovery is a cycle-time compressor and a hypothesis generator, not an oracle. It is most valuable where the DMTA loop is slow and the search space is astronomical — not where a PhD chemist already has a strong intuition.

A biotech CEO claims their "foundation model replaces medicinal chemists." Deconstruct the claim — what is plausibly true, what is marketing?

One articulation

Plausibly true: foundation models can propose novel candidate molecules faster than a human, explore chemical space more broadly, and prioritize which to synthesize — compressing the Design step of DMTA from weeks to hours. Marketing: the model does not make molecules, test them in cells, or interpret unexpected biology. The medicinal chemist's role shifts — from typing SMILES to curating, critiquing, and debugging model proposals — but does not disappear. The loop is still human-in-the-middle.

Bloom · Evaluate + Create · ~3 hrs

The autonomous atelier — integrating the loop

+

In 2020 Recursion's automated cell-imaging platform ran roughly 100,000 experiments per week. In 2023 Isomorphic Labs (DeepMind) partnered with Novartis and Lilly for multi-target discovery built on AlphaFold-inspired models. In 2024, Insilico Medicine's fully AI-designed compound entered human trials for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis. The autonomous lab is no longer science fiction. The question is how to govern it.

Day 5Learning objectives

  • Describe self-driving labs (SDLs) and the components that make them work
  • Frame the integrated target → candidate pipeline as a coupled optimization loop, not a linear sequence
  • Evaluate a discovery program's AI maturity using the 4-layer lens (Target · Hit-to-Lead · Modality · Compute)
  • Recognize the handoff from Discovery to Preclinical (pre-candidate nomination, IND-enabling studies)
  • Propose a strategic thesis: where in a discovery program is the bottleneck, and which tool attacks it?

The four key concepts

Self-driving labs

Automation (liquid handling robotics) + planning (active learning decides what to run next) + AI (models propose, score, re-plan) + instruments (assay readouts flow back without human intervention). The human sets the objective; the lab iterates autonomously.

An atelier where the blotters are smelled, rated, and re-blended by the lab itself overnight — the perfumer arrives in the morning to find fifteen new accords already triaged.

The integrated discovery loop

The four sub-disciplines are not a waterfall. Target validation informs modality choice; modality shapes what hit-finding method works; computation feeds all three; bench results reshape the original target hypothesis. Modern programs iterate across these layers monthly, not in linear phases.

A perfumer revisits the brief after the first round of blotters, then revises the ingredient organ, then retests — brief and composition co-evolve.

Handoff to preclinical

Discovery ends with candidate nomination — a specific molecule advanced for IND-enabling studies: GLP toxicology, PK/PD in relevant species, CMC manufacturing development, formulation. The baton passes to Preclinical. The candidate's TPP from Day 1 still governs the decision.

The final formula is approved, sealed, and handed to production — which then re-tests, re-validates, and sometimes demands the perfumer return.

Strategic attention — where is the bottleneck?

Every discovery program has one rate-limiting step: target uncertainty, chemical matter scarcity, developability failures, or slow DMTA cycles. Leaders diagnose which one and deploy the right tool — not every program needs a foundation model; some need a better hypothesis.

A great perfumer knows whether the problem today is the brief, the ingredients, the nose, or the weather on test-day. Not every failure is a chemistry failure.

Mentor's note

In the next decade, the discovery organizations that win will not be those with the cleverest algorithm. They will be those whose wet lab, dry lab, and clinical insight flow into one another without translation loss. The bottleneck in modern discovery is almost never compute. It is almost always the data infrastructure that lets a failed assay, a genomics signal, and a patent landscape update the model by tomorrow morning.

Your company's discovery function has a strong computational team but a slow DMTA cycle (6 weeks average). Where would you invest next — better models, automation, or organizational design — and why?

One articulation

Invest in automation + data plumbing first. A 6-week cycle is typically limited by the Make and Test steps — compound synthesis, assay turnaround, data wrangling — not the Design step. Jargon decoder · three tiers

The vocabulary of the bench.

Discovery has its own language — acronyms heavy enough to scare off outsiders. Below is the minimum working vocabulary, tiered from everyday conversation to technical meetings to specialist forums.

Tier 1 · Everyday

If you can define these, you can follow any discovery conversation
Target
The protein (usually) whose activity we want to modify. "The target is JAK1."
Hit
An early compound showing activity above a threshold. Crude, unoptimized.
Lead
A promising hit that has been improved across multiple properties and is ready for candidate optimization.
TPP
Target Product Profile — the one-page forward spec for the eventual drug.
Modality
The drug form (small mol, mAb, mRNA, etc).
ADMET
Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, Toxicity — the body's five gates.
IND
Investigational New Drug application — regulatory permission to test in humans.
Candidate
The molecule nominated to leave Discovery and enter Preclinical / IND-enabling studies.

Tier 2 · Technical

You hear these in project review meetings
HTS
High-Throughput Screening — robotic testing of ~10⁶ compounds against a target.
FBDD
Fragment-Based Drug Discovery — start from tiny molecular fragments, grow outward.
DEL
DNA-Encoded Library — 10⁹–10¹² compounds each tagged with a DNA barcode; screen in a single tube.
SAR
Structure–Activity Relationship — how structural changes shift potency/selectivity.
SBDD
Structure-Based Drug Design — designing compounds using known protein structure.
MPO
Multi-Parameter Optimization — balancing potency, selectivity, ADMET as a Pareto front.
DMTA
Design–Make–Test–Analyze cycle. The iteration unit of lead optimization.
PK / PD
Pharmacokinetics (what the body does to the drug) / Pharmacodynamics (what the drug does to the body).
CYP450
Liver enzyme family responsible for metabolizing most drugs; CYP3A4 is the workhorse.
IC50 / EC50
Concentration for 50% inhibition / effect — the standard potency unit.
ADC
Antibody-Drug Conjugate — antibody + cleavable linker + cytotoxic payload.
LNP / AAV
Lipid Nanoparticle (mRNA delivery) / Adeno-Associated Virus (gene therapy delivery vehicle).

Tier 3 · Specialist

You hear these from senior scientists and in conference posters
PROTAC
PROteolysis TArgeting Chimera — bifunctional molecule that recruits E3 ligase to degrade a target protein.
Molecular glue
Small molecule that induces a new protein–protein interaction (e.g. thalidomide, lenalidomide).
FEP / FEP+
Free-Energy Perturbation — physics-based binding affinity prediction; slow but accurate gold standard.
ESM / ProGen
Protein language models trained on millions of sequences for function and design prediction.
RFdiffusion
Diffusion-based generative model for de novo protein backbone design (Baker lab).
Cryptic pocket
A binding pocket that only appears transiently during protein motion — invisible to static structure methods.
Allosteric
Modulating a target at a site distant from its active site. Often more selective than orthosteric.
Developability
Composite score of manufacturability, stability, aggregation, immunogenicity — pre-clinical feasibility.
PAINS
Pan-Assay Interference compoundS — molecules that hit everything by promiscuous mechanism; screening false positives.
Perturb-seq
CRISPR screening + single-cell RNA-seq — reveals transcriptomic consequences of gene knockout at single-cell resolution.
Theranostic
A molecule that both treats and images disease, usually a radioligand pair (e.g. Lu-177 / Ga-68).
Traps & pitfalls

Where discovery programs quietly die.

Most discovery failures are not technical — they are epistemic. A team convinces itself a hypothesis is true before the data supports it. Six traps to recognize before they recognize you:

1

Correlation-as-target

The target's expression correlates with the disease, so the team assumes causation. Genetic evidence and functional perturbation (CRISPR knockout) are the only reliable discriminators.

Rescue: demand Mendelian randomization, rare-variant burden, or perturb-seq before committing program resources.

2

PAINS in the screen

HTS returns "hits" that actually interfere with the assay itself (redox-cycling, aggregation, fluorescence quench). Teams chase them for months.

Rescue: run orthogonal assays early, flag known PAINS substructures, insist on counter-screens.

3

The mouse-to-human leap

Stunning efficacy in a mouse model → zero response in humans. Mouse immunology, metabolism, and target pharmacology differ substantially from human.

Rescue: humanized models, organoids, iPSC-derived systems, and real patient tissue wherever possible. Temper mouse data with human genetics.

4

AlphaFold confidence fallacy

High predicted confidence (pLDDT > 90) does not mean the model is correct for your purpose. Binding pockets, dynamics, and ligand-induced conformations are outside the model's reliable zone.

Rescue: treat AlphaFold as a strong prior, not a truth. Validate experimentally (cryo-EM, HDX-MS) for pocket-level decisions.

5

Platform fetish

The team falls in love with a modality (say, mRNA or ADC) and forces every program through it, regardless of target biology. Platforms are means, not ends.

Rescue: modality-agnostic target assessment first. Platform second.

6

Optimizing the measurable

Teams optimize what they can quickly measure (cell assay IC50) and defer what they cannot (in-vivo efficacy, PK). A year in, the compound is exquisite in a dish and useless in a mouse.

Rescue: include downstream translatability gates early. Do not let a beautiful biochemistry leader mislead a program for 12 months.

Cross-domain connections

Discovery patterns echo elsewhere.

If the perfumer's atelier is the closest mirror, three other domains illuminate sub-parts of the discovery pattern. Useful when you need a fresh angle on a stuck problem.

Haute couture

Fashion design → molecular design

The sketch (concept), the toile (hit), fittings (DMTA iterations), the final fitting (candidate). Couture and discovery share the economics of one-off precision under non-negotiable constraints (the body / the biology).

Chef's table R&D

Culinary development → lead optimization

Flavor balancing across acid, salt, fat, heat, bitterness is MPO by another name. Michelin-level kitchens iterate dishes 30+ times before they hit the menu — the same cycle count as modern DMTA.

Search engineering

Information retrieval → virtual screening

Indexing billions of documents to find relevant ones is structurally the same problem as indexing chemical space to find active molecules. Many virtual-screening methods descend directly from retrieval algorithms.

Mentor's parting wisdom

You don't have to be a chemist. You do have to read the composition.

Five days cannot make you a medicinal chemist, any more than a weekend can make you a perfumer. That was never the point. The point was to give you a vocabulary and a mental model strong enough that when a discovery scientist explains their program, you can tell whether the target is well-validated, the modality well-chosen, the optimization well-framed, and the computational layer well-used.

Pharma lives and dies on the decisions made in Discovery — decisions made years before any revenue, any patient, any commercial model. The commercial side of the business is always, in the end, an interpretation of choices made at the bench.

Target first. Modality second. Chemistry third. Compute throughout. Humility always.

The molecule you will one day launch, segment, position, forecast, or price — was shaped, long before you met it, by four questions asked in an atelier: what do we bind? with what? in what form? and how fast can we learn? Learning to hear the answers to those four questions, even faintly, is the reward of this module.
