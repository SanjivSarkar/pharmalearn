§ 01 — The Hook

First, a fact that should unsettle you.

🔥 Did you know?

A modern LLM doesn't "know" a single fact. It's a ridiculously good next-word-guesser, trained on nearly everything humans have written. And yet — ask it to summarize a 20-page legal opinion and it does so beautifully. Understanding why this works, and precisely where it fails, is the single most important mental model for anyone navigating the AI era.

By Day 15, you will understand that machinery well enough to explain it over coffee — and spot the difference between genuine progress and marketing smoke.

§ 02 — The 30-Second Pitch

Agentic AI, explained like you're chatting over coffee.

The 30-second version

Modern AI is three kinds of teammate — each with different jobs, different failure modes, and different appropriate levels of oversight. The entire field can be understood as the gradual arrival of these three teammates, one after another, over the last decade.

The master analogy · referenced every phase

Your Three New Teammates

Imagine your organisation quietly hired three new teammates over the last ten years. They look nothing like each other, they have different strengths, and the field keeps confusing them — but you won't.

1

The Automator

tireless junior analyst · arrived ~2015

Follows fixed rules perfectly. Same input → same output, every time. Never tires. Never improvises. Give her a spreadsheet refresh or a weekly report and she nails it. Give her ambiguity and she seizes up. This is classic automation — RPA, workflow tools, n8n, Zapier.

2

The Augmenter

brilliant research associate · arrived ~2023

Sits at your elbow. Drafts, summarises, suggests. You keep the wheel. Reads a 40-page document and gives you a tight synthesis; drafts the email you'd have written. This is the copilot era — ChatGPT, Claude, GitHub Copilot, embedded AI in every knowledge tool.

3

The Agent

consultant with initiative · arriving ~2025–26

Give her a goal, not a task. She plans, calls tools, observes results, adjusts, and reports back. Sometimes alone, sometimes as a small team of specialists with a coordinator. This is agentic AI — the frontier of 2026.

Why this matters

Most of the confusion in AI today comes from conflating these three. "AI will take your job" usually means Teammate 1. "AI wrote my report" usually means Teammate 2. "Autonomous agents" means Teammate 3. The shape of every intelligent conversation about AI starts with asking: which teammate are we talking about?

§ 03 — The Origin Story

How all of this arrived.

In 1950, Alan Turing posed the question: can machines think? For seven decades, the answer was "not really." Researchers built expert systems (1970s), neural networks that flickered in and out of fashion (1980s–2000s), and narrow prediction models that could classify cats or forecast sales. None of them could hold a conversation.

The hinge moment came in 2017. Eight researchers at Google published a paper titled "Attention Is All You Need." They proposed a new neural architecture called the Transformer — one that, instead of reading text left-to-right, could "attend" to every word in relation to every other word, all at once. It was faster, more parallelisable, and eerily good at language.

Over the next five years, researchers discovered something surprising: if you scaled the Transformer absurdly — trillions of parameters, trained on almost the entire internet — emergent capabilities appeared. It could reason, translate, summarise, write code. By late 2022, ChatGPT brought this to the public and 100 million people tried it in two months — the fastest product adoption in history. The Augmenter had arrived.

But chat alone wasn't enough. Engineers started connecting these models to tools: letting them run code, call APIs, search the web. By 2024 a new protocol emerged — the Model Context Protocol (MCP) — standardising how any AI could talk to any tool. In parallel, models started planning multi-step actions, reflecting on outcomes, and coordinating across specialised sub-agents. That is where we stand in 2026: the Agent is walking onto the field.

1

How AI Actually Thinks (Without the Math)

⏱ Days 1–3 · ~7h 🎯 Bloom: Understand 📊
+
🔥 Curiosity Hook

An LLM doesn't "know" anything — it's a probability machine that guesses the next word, one word at a time. And yet the same mechanism, scaled to absurdity, produces something indistinguishable from reasoning. Understanding why that works (and where it breaks) is the conceptual skeleton on which every other idea in this roadmap hangs.

What you'll be able to do

  • Explain in plain English what a transformer, embedding, token, and context window are — without panicking.
  • Predict when and why an LLM will hallucinate.
  • Distinguish an LLM (text) from a diffusion model (images) from a classical ML model (prediction).
  • Describe — in one breath — the difference between foundation, fine-tuning, and prompting.

Key Concepts

📖 Large Language Model (LLM)

What it isA neural network trained on enormous amounts of text to predict the next word given everything before it. At huge scale, this simple trick starts to look like reasoning.

IntuitionThink of autocomplete on your phone, but trained on nearly the entire internet and scaled up a trillion-fold. That's essentially an LLM — just autocomplete with superpowers.

Connects toTransformers (the architecture), tokens (the "words"), context window (the working memory).

📖 Transformer

What it isThe 2017 neural-network architecture that made modern LLMs possible. Its key trick is attention — the ability to figure out which words in a sentence matter for each other, in parallel.

IntuitionPicture a dinner party where every guest can listen to every other guest simultaneously and decide in real time whose conversation matters most. That's attention. Older architectures read one word at a time; transformers read the whole table at once.

Connects toWhy LLMs are brilliant at context but notoriously bad at arithmetic.

📖 Tokens & Context Window

What it isLLMs don't see words — they see tokens (roughly three-quarters of a word each). The context window is the maximum tokens the model can hold in view at one moment. Modern models reach 200,000+ tokens — about 500 pages.

IntuitionImagine reading with a spotlight. Whatever falls inside the beam, you can see clearly. Outside it, darkness. The context window is that spotlight. Bigger spotlight = more material in view at once.

Connects toWhy RAG (Phase 4) had to be invented: you can't cram a whole library inside the spotlight.

📖 Embeddings

What it isA way of turning any piece of text (or image, or audio) into a list of numbers that encode its meaning. Similar meanings → similar number patterns.

IntuitionImagine a gigantic invisible map of meaning. "Cat" and "kitten" sit next to each other. "Cat" and "feline" are even closer. "Cat" and "tractor" live in different neighbourhoods. Embeddings are the coordinates on that map.

Connects toVector databases, semantic search, RAG (all in Phase 4).

📖 Hallucination

What it isWhen an LLM confidently produces something that isn't true. It's not lying — it's doing exactly its job (predicting plausible next words) without any access to ground truth.

IntuitionImagine a very confident writer who has read everything but never cross-checks the date. They'll cheerfully invent a plausible-sounding citation. That's hallucination — a structural feature of pattern-matching, not a bug that will simply be "fixed."

Connects toRAG (reduces it), evaluations (measures it), guardrails (contains it).

📖 Foundation vs Fine-Tuned vs Prompted

What it isThree ways to get an LLM to do what you want. Foundation = use the raw model as-is. Fine-tuning = retrain it on your data (slow, expensive). Prompting = give it better instructions (fast, cheap, usually enough).

IntuitionSame brilliant generalist. Foundation = hire them as they are. Fine-tune = send them to specialist school. Prompt = brief them well. Nine times out of ten, the good brief beats the extra school.

Connects toPrompt engineering (Phase 3), RAG (Phase 4).

🧠 Mentor's Signature Analogy

Imagine you hired a savant intern who spent her childhood locked in the world's largest library. She read everything ever published — every book, article, manual, poem — up through a certain month. She remembers nothing personal, nothing after her cutoff date, and has no way to check what's true right now. But give her any question in language she's seen and she produces a brilliant, confident reply.

That is an LLM. Absurdly well-read. No long-term memory. Brilliant at generating plausible output. Occasionally confident-but-wrong (that's hallucination). The rest of this roadmap is essentially about giving that intern a library card (RAG), a notebook (memory), hands (tools), and a boss (the agent loop).

What it reveals: every future concept in this roadmap — RAG, fine-tuning, agents, tools — is an answer to one of these three failure modes. You now have the skeleton of the whole field on one page of paper.

Automation — The First Teammate

⏱ Days 4–6 · ~7h 🎯 Bloom: Understand + Analyze 📊
+
🔥 Curiosity Hook

Most of what gets marketed as "AI" in 2026 is actually not AI at all — it's plain old automation with a language-model cherry on top. Once you can separate the deterministic scaffolding from the probabilistic "brain," you stop being dazzled by demos and start seeing the architecture underneath. That skill alone is worth the entire roadmap.

What you'll be able to do

  • Explain the difference between deterministic automation and probabilistic AI — with a real example for each.
  • Sketch any workflow as three Lego bricks: Trigger → Action → Condition.
  • Tell when a task should NOT use AI — and why that judgement is a senior skill.
  • Recognise when a flashy "AI product" is really just an n8n/Zapier workflow with an LLM step inside.

Key Concepts

📖 Deterministic vs Probabilistic

What it isDeterministic = same input, same output, every single time (Excel formula, SQL query). Probabilistic = same input can produce slightly different outputs (any LLM). This single distinction drives half of all AI architecture decisions.

IntuitionA vending machine is deterministic — press B4, get the same chocolate bar every time. A waiter recommending a dish is probabilistic — same question, different answer depending on mood, inventory, and memory. You want the vending machine for billing; the waiter for suggestions.

Connects toWhy some tasks should never touch an LLM, no matter how impressive the demo.

📖 Workflow Automation Platforms

What it isTools that let you wire up "when X happens, do Y" without writing code. Triggers, actions, and some branching logic. Popular examples: n8n, Zapier, Make, Microsoft Power Automate.

IntuitionThink of the old Rube Goldberg cartoons — one marble triggers a domino, which trips a mousetrap, which pours the cereal. Workflow platforms are the digital version. You connect pre-built blocks; the system runs when conditions fire.

Connects toThe invisible backbone of 80% of "AI products" you see on social media.

📖 RPA vs AI Automation

What it isRPA (Robotic Process Automation) = a bot that mimics a human clicking buttons in a user interface. AI automation = an LLM that understands intent and adapts to small variations. Modern stacks use both together.

IntuitionRPA is like a player piano — it follows the roll perfectly, forever, but never improvises. AI automation is a jazz musician — it can riff when the moment calls for it. Put them in the same band and you can cover most repertoire.

Connects toWhy legacy RPA vendors (UiPath, Blue Prism) are scrambling to add "agentic" capabilities.

📖 Triggers, Actions & Conditions

What it isThe three Lego bricks of every workflow. Trigger = the event that starts the flow. Action = what the flow does. Condition = optional branching logic ("if this, then that, else the other").

IntuitionA smoke alarm: Trigger = smoke detected, Condition = level above threshold, Action = sound + notify. Every automation, from email filters to enterprise AI, is some flavour of these three.

Connects toHow you'll mentally sketch every automation idea for the rest of your career.

📖 When NOT to Use AI

What it isA judgement skill, not a technical one. If a task has 100% correctness requirements, deterministic rules, or legal exposure — use classical automation or code, not an LLM. Probabilistic tools for probabilistic problems only.

IntuitionWould you ask a poet to calculate your taxes? Great poet, wrong tool. Would you ask a calculator to write a condolence note? Accurate numbers, wrong tool. Matching tool to task is the most underrated senior skill in the AI era.

Connects toAlmost every governance discussion in Phase 5.

🧠 Mentor's Signature Analogy

Remember your three teammates? The Automator is Monday-morning-junior-analyst personified. Same spreadsheet, same rules, same deadline — she nails it, every time, forever, without complaint. But give her something new, something that requires judgement, and she either seizes up or does something wrong with great confidence.

Industries burned billions asking classical automation bots to do work that required judgement. The lesson is sharp: match the teammate to the task. Routine, rule-based, high-volume → Automator. Ambiguous, judgement-heavy, low-volume → wait for Teammate 2 or 3. Over the next phases you'll meet both, and you'll learn how to decide which to call.

Copilots & the Craft of Prompting

⏱ Days 7–9 · ~7h 🎯 Bloom: Apply 📊
+
🔥 Curiosity Hook

The difference between a mediocre LLM answer and a brilliant one is often about two hundred extra characters in the prompt. Prompting, it turns out, is mostly just good briefing — the same skill any manager uses when delegating to a new hire. The people who seem to get "magic" out of AI aren't using secret tools; they're writing clearer briefs.

What you'll be able to do

  • Recognise and apply the CRAFT prompt pattern — the five dials every prompt has.
  • Choose between few-shot prompting, chain-of-thought, and role-based prompting for the right task.
  • Explain the difference between a system prompt and a user prompt in one breath.
  • Define a "copilot" and distinguish it from an "agent" — a distinction that will matter hugely in Phase 5.

Key Concepts

📖 The CRAFT Prompt Pattern

What it isFive dials on every good prompt. Context (the situation), Role (who the AI should be), Action (what exactly to do), Format (how the output should look), Target (who the audience is).

Intuition"Write a summary" = 😐. "You are a veteran science journalist [Role]. A curious high-schooler [Target] just asked about CRISPR [Context]. Write a three-paragraph explanation [Action] using the 'problem → mechanism → implications' structure [Format]" = 🎯. Same model, completely different quality.

Connects toEvery future interaction you'll have with any LLM.

📖 System Prompt vs User Prompt

What it isSystem prompt = the AI's "job description," persistent across a conversation. User prompt = each message you send. Custom GPTs, Claude Projects, and Gemini Gems are really just saved system prompts with context files attached.

IntuitionThink of hiring a contractor. The contract (system prompt) sets tone, boundaries, and general expectations for the whole relationship. Each task assignment (user prompt) happens within that frame. Change the contract and the nature of every task changes with it.

Connects toWhy a configured "Project" feels so much smarter than plain chat.

📖 Few-Shot Prompting

What it isGiving the model 2-5 examples of good input-output pairs before your actual question. The model spots the pattern and follows it.

IntuitionImagine training a new assistant. "Classify these emails" is weak. "Here are three classified emails as examples — now do these twenty" is dramatically stronger. You've shown the pattern instead of described it. Models, like humans, learn better from worked examples.

Connects toA 30-50% accuracy boost on classification tasks, essentially for free.

📖 Chain-of-Thought Prompting

What it isAsking the model to "think step by step" before answering. The request itself triggers more methodical reasoning in the model's output.

IntuitionAsk a student "what's 24 × 17?" and they may guess. Say "show your working" and you force a slower, more accurate path through the problem. Same phenomenon in LLMs — explicit reasoning produces better answers on multi-step tasks.

Connects toThe internal reasoning loop that, when taken further, becomes agentic behaviour (Phase 5).

📖 Copilot

What it isAn AI that lives inside your existing workflow — drafting, suggesting, completing — while you keep the wheel. GitHub Copilot in an editor. Claude in Excel. Microsoft 365 Copilot across Office.

IntuitionPicture your three teammates again. The copilot is Teammate 2 — the research associate at your elbow. She proposes; you decide. She drafts; you edit. You never leave your tool, but your throughput changes shape.

Connects toThe defining shape of "Augmentation" — and what separates a copilot from an agent in Phase 5.

📖 Skills / Projects / Custom GPTs

What it isPackaged bundles of prompt + context + reference documents, saved for repeat use. Claude Skills, Claude Projects, ChatGPT Custom GPTs, Gemini Gems, NotebookLM notebooks — all variations on the same idea.

IntuitionIf a prompt is a briefing, a saved Skill is a whole role with a binder of reference material. You prepare it once, and every future conversation inside it inherits that preparation. This is how prompting scales.

Connects toReusability of prompting work across a team or community.

🧠 Mentor's Signature Analogy

Recall the three teammates. The Augmenter — brilliant research associate, sitting at your elbow — is extraordinary. But here's the thing about new hires: they do what you say. Say something vague, you'll get a vague answer. Say something precise, you'll get a precise one.

Prompting is just briefing a new teammate well. Context, role, the actual ask, the format wanted, the audience. That's it. You already do this every time you delegate to a junior — you're just now doing it in writing, to a teammate who types at two hundred words per second, works through lunch, and never complains. The quality of your briefing sets the ceiling of everything that follows.

◎
Visualisation Exercise: The Five Dials
Type: mental sketch · Time: 10 minutes · Materials: paper
  1. Draw five dials in a row, like volume knobs on an audio mixer. Label them, left to right: Context · Role · Action · Format · Target.
  2. Pick any generic prompt you might have typed — "write me an email," say. Write it at the top of the page.
  3. Now mentally turn each dial. For Context: add the situation. For Role: specify who the AI should be. For Action: sharpen the verb. For Format: describe structure. For Target: name the audience.
  4. Rewrite the prompt with all five dials "turned up." Notice how long it's become — and how much more useful the output would be.
  5. Repeat with two more prompts from your week. By the third one, CRAFT will be automatic.
What it reveals: "prompt engineering" isn't a mystical skill — it's briefing, explicit and disciplined. Once the five dials are in your hand, prompts stop being incantations and become controls.

RAG, Vector DBs & MCP — Grounding AI in Reality

⏱ Days 10–12 · ~7h 🎯 Bloom: Analyze 📊
+
🔥 Curiosity Hook

If Phase 1 is the brain, this phase is the nervous system. RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is how we stop LLMs from making things up by giving them a library card. MCP (Model Context Protocol) is the standard plug — launched in late 2024 — that lets any AI model talk to any tool or data source. Together these two ideas are quietly the most important architectural shift in enterprise AI since the transformer itself.

What you'll be able to do

  • Draw the RAG pipeline from memory — the four steps from question to grounded answer.
  • Explain in plain words what a vector database does and why it was invented.
  • Describe MCP as the "USB-C for AI" — and name one thing it made possible that was hard before.
  • Explain why evaluations ("evals") are the unglamorous foundation of any trustworthy AI system.

Key Concepts

📖 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

What it isBefore answering, the system retrieves relevant chunks of your documents and hands them to the LLM as context. The LLM then generates an answer grounded in those chunks, often with citations. The canonical four-step pipeline: chunk → embed → retrieve → generate.

IntuitionA brilliant but forgetful friend at a trivia night. Before she answers, a research assistant slips her a handful of index cards with the relevant facts. Now her answer is not only confident — it's correct, and she can point to which card it came from.

Connects toHallucination (Phase 1) — RAG is the primary structural defence against it.

📖 Chunking

What it isThe unglamorous but critical step of splitting your documents into smaller retrievable passages — typically a few hundred words each — before embedding them.

IntuitionImagine trying to find a single paragraph by rummaging through an entire bookshelf. Impossible. Now imagine someone has torn the books into index-card-sized pieces, each tagged with its origin. Suddenly findable. That's chunking — and bad chunking is one of the top three reasons RAG systems fail.

Connects toVector databases, which store and search those chunks.

📖 Vector Database

What it isA specialised database optimised for finding items with similar meaning — not matching keywords. It stores embeddings (Phase 1) and performs blazingly fast "find similar" lookups. Popular examples: Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, pgvector.

IntuitionA traditional database is like a dictionary — exact words, exact matches. A vector database is like a friend who understands what you mean — ask for "affordable hotels" and she pulls up ones priced as "budget-friendly," "cheap," and "good value" without being told they're synonyms.

Connects toHow RAG retrieves the right chunks in milliseconds, even across millions of documents.

📖 Model Context Protocol (MCP)

What it isAn open standard, launched by Anthropic in November 2024, that lets any AI model talk to any data source or tool through a common protocol. Tools and data expose themselves through an MCP "server"; any MCP-capable model can then use them.

IntuitionBefore USB-C, every phone had its own charger shape. MCP is USB-C for AI. Instead of building fifty custom integrations (this model ↔ that tool, this model ↔ this other tool), you build one MCP server per tool, and any MCP-capable model plugs in.

Connects toTool use (Phase 5) — MCP is how tool use gets standardised and scaled.

📖 Semantic Layer / Ontology

What it isA translation layer that teaches an AI system your organisation's vocabulary — what specific terms mean in your context, how categories are defined, which synonyms refer to what.

IntuitionImagine hiring a new employee. Without an internal glossary, they'll use "customer" and "client" interchangeably when the company distinguishes them carefully. A semantic layer is the glossary — boring to build, invisible when working, disastrous when absent.

Connects toWhy RAG alone is not enough for enterprise systems; meaning must be shared, not just matched.

📖 Evaluations ("Evals")

What it isTest suites that measure whether an AI system is actually producing good answers. Like regression tests for software, but for language. Hundreds of labelled examples, pass/fail criteria, run regularly as the system changes.

IntuitionIf you can't measure it, you can't improve it — and you certainly can't trust it in production. Evals are the thermometer. Every credible AI system has them; every untrustworthy one doesn't. "Show me your evals" is the single best diagnostic question to ask any AI team or vendor.

Connects toGovernance — without evals, there is no governance, only hope.

🧠 Mentor's Signature Analogy

Remember the savant intern with no personal memory? RAG is giving her a library card. Before she answers each question, a librarian (the retriever) runs to the stacks (the vector database), pulls the three most relevant passages, and drops them on her desk. She reads, synthesises, cites. The hallucinating intern becomes the intern who says: "let me check, and get back to you."

And MCP? MCP is the standardisation of how she talks to every department. Before MCP, every department had its own phone system, its own jargon, its own forms. After MCP, one protocol. She can walk into any office — yours, finance, the warehouse, any external service — and ask the same kind of question in the same way. The integration tax plummets, and new tools become usable almost for free.

Agentic AI & Multi-Agent Systems

⏱ Days 13–15 · ~7h 🎯 Bloom: Evaluate 📊

🔥 Curiosity Hook

The shift from copilots (2023-24) to agents (2025-26) is, as one analyst put it, "structurally different." An agent is no longer a productivity layer — it's an operational participant. You no longer ask it "what do you think?" You say "handle this by Friday" and walk away. Understanding what makes an AI "agentic" is the conceptual frontier of 2026 — and once you see the pattern, you'll never un-see it.

Key Concepts

📖 What Makes AI "Agentic"?

What it isThree properties, all required: (1) goal-directed (given an objective, not a question), (2) tool-using (can call APIs, run code, query data, act on the world), (3) iterative (plans, acts, observes, reflects, continues until done). Remove any one and it's not an agent.

Intuition"What's the weather?" — not agentic. "Plan my trip to Rome, book the flight, add calendar invites, and brief me the night before" — agentic. The first wants an answer. The second wants an outcome.

Connects toWhy the same underlying LLM can be a copilot or an agent depending on what you wrap around it.

📖 The ReAct Loop

What it isThe canonical four-step architecture that runs inside every agent: Reason → Act → Observe → Reflect → repeat, until the goal is met (or the agent gives up). This loop is the beating heart of agentic behaviour.

IntuitionWatch a good detective work. They reason ("the butler might have been in the library"), act (question him), observe (his alibi checks out), reflect ("so it can't be him — who else had access?"), and loop again. Agents do exactly this, at machine speed. Crucially, agents can recover from errors precisely because they observe and adjust.

Connects toWhy agents are resilient to small failures in a way pipelines never were.

📖 Tool Use / Function Calling

What it isThe mechanism by which an LLM actually does things. You give the model a menu of tools (functions with their inputs, outputs, and descriptions). It decides which to call, with what inputs, and in what order.

IntuitionThink of a chef in a kitchen surrounded by labelled tools — whisk, mixer, oven, knife. The chef reads the recipe (goal), picks the right tool for each step, observes the result, picks the next tool. An LLM with tools is a chef with a kitchen. Without tools, it's a chef at a writing desk.

Connects toMCP (Phase 4) is the standardised language for declaring these tools.

📖 Multi-Agent Orchestration

What it isMultiple specialised agents — each great at one thing — coordinated by an orchestrator (sometimes called a "super-agent" or "supervisor"). Modular, testable, bounded. Critical for tasks too complex for a single agent to hold in mind.

IntuitionA consulting engagement. The project manager decomposes the problem, assigns sub-problems to specialists (researcher, analyst, writer), and integrates their findings. Multi-agent systems are that structure, rendered in software. Specialisation beats generalisation — and orchestration beats chaos.

Connects toFrameworks that implement this: LangGraph, Claude Agent SDK, CrewAI, AutoGen.

📖 Agent Autonomy Levels (0-5)

What it isAn industry maturity scale borrowed in spirit from self-driving cars. L0 = fully manual. L1 = AI suggests. L2 = AI acts with approval. L3 = AI acts with checkpoints. L4 = goal-directed, human-on-the-loop. L5 = fully autonomous multi-agent. Most production systems today sit at L2-L3.

IntuitionThe autonomy level is the amount of leash. Short leash: every action approved. Medium leash: big decisions approved. Long leash: report the outcome, not the steps. Vendors love to claim L5; real systems rarely earn it — and often shouldn't.

Connects toThe single most important architectural choice in any agent system.

📖 Agent Guardrails

What it isHard limits on what an agent can do. Tool permissions, rate limits, approval gates for irreversible actions, audit logs, defences against prompt injection. The things that survive an audit.

IntuitionA new employee's security badge. It opens certain doors, not others. Audit logs remember every swipe. Some doors require a second person. Without this, you simply cannot let agents near anything important. With it, you can.

Connects toWhy "agents in production" is mostly a governance story wearing a technical hat.

📖 Agent Frameworks

What it isLibraries for building agents. LangChain = general toolbox, the early workhorse. LangGraph = LangChain's modern graph-based successor for complex multi-agent flows. Claude Agent SDK = Anthropic's opinionated, production-oriented alternative. CrewAI, AutoGen = role-based multi-agent frameworks. Similar concepts, different ergonomics.

IntuitionThese are kitchens, not recipes. Each lays out the tools in a different order, each opinionated about how multi-agent conversations should be structured. Pick one and learn its idioms deeply; the concepts transfer across all of them.

Connects toThe frameworks your engineering team will argue about next quarter.

🧠 Mentor's Signature Analogy

The Agent — our third teammate, the consultant with initiative — is structurally different from the first two. You don't say "refresh this report" (the Automator) or "draft this section" (the Augmenter). You say "figure out why this trend reversed and come back with three options by Friday." Then you walk away.

Behind the scenes, the consultant does what good consultants do: plans the investigation, calls sources (databases, documents, tools), gets answers, updates the plan, tries again, writes up findings. A multi-agent system is the same consultant bringing along specialists — a researcher, an analyst, a writer — coordinated by a project manager. Same shape, more specialisation.

The senior question isn't "should we use agents?" It's "at what autonomy level, for which task, with what guardrails?" That single reframe will separate you from 90% of conversations about agents you'll overhear in the coming year.

§ 07 — The Jargon Decoder

Every buzzword, in plain English.

Keep this page open in a tab during every conversation about AI for the next month. You'll be astonished how often these fifteen terms come up — and how often they're used incorrectly.

Foundational · Phases 1–2
LLM

Textbook: A neural network trained to predict the next token in a sequence.

Plain: An absurdly well-read intern who guesses the next word really, really well.

Remember: guesses words — doesn't "know" facts.

Transformer

Textbook: Neural architecture using self-attention, introduced by Vaswani et al., 2017.

Plain: The engine under every LLM's hood. One paper invented it.

Remember: it's an architecture — not a big robot.

Token

Textbook: The atomic unit of LLM input and output.

Plain: Roughly three-quarters of a word. LLMs "see" in tokens.

Remember: 500 pages ≈ 200,000 tokens.

Context Window

Textbook: Maximum tokens an LLM can process in a single call.

Plain: The AI's working memory — what it can see at once.

Remember: the spotlight in the library.

Hallucination

Textbook: Generation of plausible-but-factually-wrong content.

Plain: Confidently making things up. Structural feature, not a bug.

Remember: LLMs are plausibility engines, not truth engines.

Deterministic

Textbook: Same input → same output, every time.

Plain: Predictable. SQL, spreadsheets, classical code.

Remember: deterministic for routine, AI for ambiguity.

RPA

Textbook: Robotic Process Automation — software bots mimicking UI actions.

Plain: A bot pretending to be a human clicking buttons.

Remember: RPA ≠ AI. They're complements, not rivals.

Intermediate · Phases 3–4
Prompt Engineering

Textbook: Designing inputs to optimise LLM outputs.

Plain: Briefing your AI teammate clearly.

Remember: CRAFT — Context, Role, Action, Format, Target.

System Prompt

Textbook: Persistent instructions at the start of a conversation.

Plain: The AI's job description. Set once, applies throughout.

Remember: saved Projects and Custom GPTs are system prompts with attachments.

Few-Shot

Textbook: Including 2-5 input-output examples in the prompt.

Plain: "Here are three worked examples. Now do this one."

Remember: biggest free accuracy boost on classification tasks.

Chain-of-Thought

Textbook: Prompting the model to reason step-by-step before answering.

Plain: "Show your working, then give me the answer."

Remember: big gains on multi-step reasoning problems.

Copilot

Textbook: AI assistant embedded in an existing user workflow.

Plain: Research associate at your elbow. You drive.

Remember: Teammate 2 in the three-teammate model.

Embedding

Textbook: Dense vector representation of semantic meaning.

Plain: Coordinates on an invisible map of meaning.

Remember: similar meanings sit close together.

Vector Database

Textbook: Database optimised for similarity search over embeddings.

Plain: Storage with fast "find similar meaning" lookup.

Remember: Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, pgvector.

RAG

Textbook: Retrieval-Augmented Generation — retrieve, then generate.

Plain: Give the LLM a library card before it answers.

Remember: chunk → embed → retrieve → generate.

Chunking

Textbook: Splitting documents into retrievable passages before embedding.

Plain: Tearing documents into index-card-sized pieces.

Remember: bad chunking = bad RAG. Don't skip.

MCP

Textbook: Model Context Protocol — open standard for LLM-tool connectivity.

Plain: USB-C for AI. One plug for every system.

Remember: launched Nov 2024 by Anthropic. Now industry default.

Fine-Tuning

Textbook: Additional training to adjust model weights on domain-specific data.

Plain: Sending the AI to a specialist school. Costly. Often unnecessary.

Remember: try prompting + RAG first. Always.

Evals

Textbook: Test suites quantifying AI output quality.

Plain: Regression tests for language. The thermometer.

Remember: "show me your evals" = the magic diagnostic phrase.

Advanced · Phase 5
Agent

Textbook: Goal-directed AI that plans, uses tools, and iterates.

Plain: Junior consultant — give it a goal, walk away.

Remember: Goal + Tools + Loop. All three required.

ReAct Loop

Textbook: Reason → Act → Observe → Reflect, iterated until done.

Plain: The heartbeat of every agent. Four-step cycle.

Remember: agents recover from errors because they observe them.

Tool Use / Function Calling

Textbook: LLM invokes external functions via structured calls.

Plain: The AI's hands. How it acts on the world.

Remember: MCP is the standardised language for this.

Multi-Agent Orchestration

Textbook: Coordination of specialised agents by an orchestrator.

Plain: A consulting team with a PM coordinating specialists.

Remember: LangGraph, Claude Agent SDK, CrewAI, AutoGen.

Autonomy Levels (L0–L5)

Textbook: Industry maturity scale for AI decision latitude.

Plain: How much leash the agent gets.

Remember: most responsible production systems sit at L2-L3.

Guardrails

Textbook: Hard constraints, permission boundaries, approval gates.

Plain: What the agent cannot do, written in ink.

Remember: guardrailed = auditable.

Textbook: Malicious input designed to override model instructions.

Plain: An attacker smuggling instructions through data.

Remember: the key agent-era vulnerability — defend with guardrails.

Human-in-the-Loop

Textbook: Architecture mandating human review at defined points.

Plain: Checkpoints where a human decides.

Remember: non-negotiable for any high-stakes system.

§ 08 — Pitfalls & Misconceptions

Five traps almost everyone walks into.

These are not the rare edge cases. These are the misconceptions you will encounter in nearly every article, vendor pitch, and hallway conversation about AI. Memorising the reality side of each row is half the work of being useful in an AI conversation.

TRAP 01 "AI actually understands what it's saying."
MythThe model genuinely comprehends meaning and intent the way a person does.
RealityThe model is a pattern-matcher trained on enormous text. It produces outputs that look like understanding because so much of human understanding is itself pattern-matching — but the underlying mechanism is statistical next-token prediction, not comprehension.
AvoidHold both ideas at once: the outputs are genuinely impressive and the mechanism is simpler than it appears. Drop either half and you'll misjudge when to trust it.
TRAP 02 "You need to fine-tune your own LLM."
MythTo get good results on your own domain, you must fine-tune a custom model.
Reality~95% of real-world use cases are solved with good prompting plus RAG. Fine-tuning is expensive, brittle, slow to update, and usually inferior. Vendors push it because it sounds serious and earns revenue.
AvoidDefault to prompting + RAG. Demand anyone proposing fine-tuning show side-by-side evaluations against a well-prompted baseline first.
TRAP 03 "Agents are the future — skip everything else."
MythAutomation and copilots are obsolete. Go straight to agents.
RealityThe overwhelming majority of near-term value lives in plain automation and copilots. Agents look magnificent in demos and fail dramatically in production without mature governance. Mature teams build portfolios, not favourites — a mix of all three teammates.
AvoidInsist on understanding all three teammates. The boring automation wins fund the interesting agent experiments.
TRAP 04 "If it uses AI, it's cutting-edge."
MythAn "AI-powered" product must be architecturally modern.
RealityA large share of "AI-powered" demos on the internet are a workflow platform (Zapier, n8n, Make) with a single LLM step inside. Nothing wrong with that — but it's not sorcery. Ask about architecture, evaluations, and how it handles failure.
AvoidApply three questions to every pitch: what category of AI is this (Automation / Augmentation / Agent)? What happens when the LLM is wrong? Where are the evals?
TRAP 05 "Hallucination is solved in the new models."
MythNewer, larger models have eliminated hallucination.
RealityHallucination is reduced, not eliminated. It is a structural property of next-token prediction — a pattern engine with no access to ground truth will occasionally produce plausible-but-wrong output. RAG, guardrails, and evaluations manage it. Nothing yet eliminates it.
AvoidDesign systems for when they hallucinate, not if. Any vendor claiming zero hallucination is either misunderstanding the problem or misrepresenting their system.
META-TRAP "I should understand all this perfectly before I speak about it."
MythYou need to read fifty papers before you can hold an opinion.
RealityEveryone is reasoning with incomplete mental models — including experts. Your edge isn't completeness; it's clarity of framing (the three teammates, the ReAct loop, the RAG pipeline) plus the humility to say "I don't know — let me check" on specifics. Frames beat facts in conversation.
AvoidStart articulating these ideas aloud by Day 7. Your Phase 1-2 foundation is already more than most people have. Iterate in public.
§ 09 — Cross-Domain Connections

The same ideas, in other fields.

Understanding is built through pathways. Seeing the same patterns in different domains cements them in your head — and gives you multiple retrieval routes for when someone mentions one in passing.

§

Software Engineering

Coding is where every agentic idea was first battle-tested. Claude Code, Cursor, and GitHub Copilot Agent are canonical examples: a goal ("fix this bug"), tools (read/write files, run commands), iteration (test, observe, adjust). If you want to watch the ReAct loop happen in real time, watch an agentic coding tool work on a small task. You're seeing the future of every agent.

§

Autonomous Vehicles

The autonomy levels we use for agents (L0 through L5) were borrowed directly from self-driving cars. The analogy is deliberate. L2 cars ("hands-off highway driving, eyes on the road") correspond exactly to L2 agents ("acts with approval"). The engineering lessons about graceful degradation, clear hand-offs to humans, and extensive testing transfer almost verbatim to AI agent design.

§

Scientific Research

What does a great researcher actually do? Form a hypothesis (reason), design an experiment (act), check the result (observe), update the hypothesis (reflect), and repeat. That's the ReAct loop, invented by scientists long before it had a name. When you see an agent operating, you're watching a machine-speed miniature of the scientific method.

§

Human Memory & Libraries

Every idea in this roadmap has an analogue in how humans handle knowledge. Long-term memory = model weights. Working memory = the context window. Books we check = RAG retrieval. Tools we use = function calling. The structural similarity isn't coincidence — AI systems are, to a surprising degree, externalised versions of the cognitive architectures we already use.

These connections mean your brain now has several routes to recall the same ideas. When someone mentions a driverless car's L3 autonomy, you'll pattern-match to agent autonomy instantly. That's memory, working the way memory is supposed to.
