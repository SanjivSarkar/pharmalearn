Module 01 · Strategy & BD&L · 5-day Roadmap

The Strategist's Map for Pharma

A five-day theoretical tour through the brain of a pharmaceutical company — how it chooses its battlefields, sizes its bets, hunts for the right deals, and reads its rivals.

The Hook
Did you know?

Of every 10,000 compounds that enter a pharma company's discovery pipeline, roughly one makes it to the pharmacy shelf — and that one journey takes 10–15 years and $2.6 billion. Strategy and BD&L exist to decide which tiny fraction of those 10,000 even deserve the attention in the first place.

The 30-Second Premise

Pharma strategy is deciding what to bet on, how much, and from where.

Every pharmaceutical company faces the same four questions, continuously, for every asset in its portfolio and every dollar in its budget. These four questions are the four pillars of Module 1 — and they never stop being asked, from the day a chemist first draws a molecule until decades later, long after the patent expires.

The Master Analogy → Pharma as a Top-Tier Football Club

Imagine you are the director of football at a club like Manchester City or Real Madrid. You don't kick a ball. You don't pick the starting eleven. You do something far more consequential: you decide which competitions the club will try to win, which players to develop, which transfers to pursue, and what rival clubs are plotting.

A pharmaceutical company is exactly the same institution — just with scientists instead of strikers, therapeutic areas instead of tournaments, and drug candidates instead of footballers. Every concept in this five-day roadmap maps back to this one picture.

Why This Field Exists

The origin story — how pharma learned to think strategically.

For most of the twentieth century, pharmaceutical companies were chemistry houses. Scientists ran screens, molecules emerged, and a relatively small commercial team took whatever chemistry found and sold it hard. Strategy, as a discipline, barely existed.

That world broke in the 1990s and 2000s. Two forces collided. First, the cost of developing a drug climbed past a billion dollars per approval — failure was no longer an absorbable accident. Second, the patent cliff arrived: blockbuster drugs like Lipitor and Plavix lost exclusivity almost simultaneously, wiping out tens of billions of dollars of annual revenue in a single year. Suddenly, deciding which diseases to pursue, which assets to buy, and which to kill mattered more than any one molecule.

From that pressure, a new function crystallized. Corporate strategy started asking which therapeutic areas were worth a 15-year commitment. Portfolio management brought the discipline of probability-weighted valuation to the pipeline. BD&L (business development & licensing) transformed from a sleepy deal desk into the single largest source of new assets for most large pharma — it is common today for more than half of a major company's revenue to come from drugs it did not originally discover. And competitive intelligence professionalized into an always-on radar scanning trial readouts, conference abstracts, and patent filings in real time.

This five-day map walks you through all four — in the order they tend to be asked inside a real company.

I

Day 1 · The Landscape

~3 hours Bloom's: Remember → Understand Foundation
+
Curiosity Hook

A blockbuster drug that sells $5 billion a year can take 15 years of strategy work before it ever shows up in a TV ad — and most of that strategy happens before the first patient is even dosed.

What you'll be able to do

  • Describe the 10 chronological phases of a drug's life, from Discovery to Post-LOE.
  • Explain why Strategy & BD&L is "always on" — active in every one of those 10 phases.
  • Name the four pillars of Module 1 and what question each one answers.
  • Use the master analogy (the football club) to orient any new concept you meet.

Key Concepts

Foundation

The 10-Phase Lifecycle

A drug's life is typically broken into ten chronological phases: Discovery → Preclinical → Phase 1 → Phase 2 → Phase 3 → Regulatory Submission → Launch → In-Line Growth → LOE Defense → Post-LOE. Each phase has its own dominant concerns and its own dominant functional owners.

Like a football career: youth academy → reserves → first-team debut → breakthrough season → peak years → testimonial → transfer out → retirement. Same person, different questions at each stage.

Connects to → Every other concept in this roadmap lives somewhere on this timeline.

Foundation

Strategy is "Always On"

Unlike Clinical Operations (which peaks during Phase 3) or Manufacturing (which peaks at Launch), strategy and BD&L run continuously — from Day 0 of Discovery to the final Post-LOE wind-down. It is one of very few functions that never sleep.

The club's director of football is never on holiday. There's always a transfer window somewhere, an academy prospect to evaluate, a rival's tactics to decipher.

Connects to → Why portfolio management and CI are continuous disciplines, not one-off projects.

Foundation

The Four Pillars

Everything in Module 1 sits under one of four questions: (1) What fields do we compete in? (Corporate & TA Strategy, Day 2). (2) What do we already have, and what is it worth? (Portfolio Management, Day 3). (3) What should we buy, sell, or license? (BD&L, Day 4). (4) What are our rivals doing? (Competitive Intelligence, Day 5).

These are literally the four questions every football director faces every morning: What leagues are we targeting? What squad do I have? Who should I sign or sell? What are City, United, Liverpool doing?

Connects to → Days 2–5 are one pillar each.

Foundation

Dominant Functions per Phase

Each lifecycle phase has a different set of dominant functions. Discovery belongs to Research and IP. Phase 3 belongs to Clinical Operations, Biostatistics, and HEOR. Launch belongs to Commercial, Market Access, and PV. Strategy is present in all of them — but it always speaks through whichever function is currently dominant.

Youth-academy season → coaches dominate. Pre-season → fitness staff dominate. Transfer window → director of football dominates. The director influences all seasons, but speaks with different voices.

Connects to → How strategy translates into action depends entirely on the phase you are in.

Mentor's Signature Analogy — Day 1

Picture the tunnel of a football stadium before kickoff. The manager is pacing. The physio is checking the left-back's knee. The analysts are queuing up the match-day tactical board. Where is the director of football? Not in the tunnel. She's in the directors' box, already on the phone — because the January transfer window opens in eight weeks, the U-19s have a match tonight, and the rival's star striker is rumoured to be unhappy.

That's pharma strategy. Everyone else works a shift. The strategist is never off the clock, because the next drug's fate is being decided somewhere in the shadows of every single day.

"Why is Strategy & BD&L drawn as a single long bar across all ten phases, while Clinical Development is a short bar that peaks and then tapers off?"

Think about who is asking which drug should exist at all versus who is asking does this specific drug work.

II

Day 2 · Corporate & TA Strategy

~3.5 hours Bloom's: Understand → Apply Core
+
Curiosity Hook

Two of the ten largest pharmas of 1995 no longer exist as independent companies. The usual cause of death was not a single failed drug — it was a wrong bet on which therapeutic areas would matter over the next decade.

What you'll be able to do

  • Distinguish the four dominant strategic archetypes (specialty, rare-disease, oncology-first, diversified).
  • Apply the north-star framework: mission → TA mandate → asset priorities.
  • Score a therapeutic area on the four classical TA-selection criteria.
  • Reason about breadth vs. depth and when a company should exit a TA.

Key Concepts

Foundation

Strategic Archetypes

Pharma companies tend to cluster into four recognizable shapes. Specialty: narrow TA focus, high-science, high-price (e.g. rare neurology). Rare disease: very small populations, very high prices per patient, orphan-drug incentives. Oncology-first: an enormous pipeline concentrated in cancer, often on a handful of platforms. Diversified: many TAs, many modalities, many revenue streams. Each archetype has its own economics, its own risk profile, and its own characteristic investor expectation.

Football clubs cluster the same way: a youth-academy club (Ajax), a Galácticos club (Real Madrid), a tactical-system club (old-school Barcelona), or a franchise chain (City Football Group). The archetype dictates every subsequent choice.

Connects to → Day 3 portfolio construction, Day 4 which deals even get looked at.

Core

The North-Star Framework

A three-tier cascade. At the top: Mission (e.g. "double the life of patients with incurable cancers"). One level down: TA Mandate — the specific therapeutic areas you commit to, and, crucially, the ones you explicitly do not commit to. Bottom level: Asset Priorities — which individual programs in those TAs get resources first.

Mission: "Win the Champions League every decade." TA Mandate: "UEFA competitions + domestic league only; no cup runs at the expense of league form." Asset Priorities: "Striker and centre-back this summer; goalkeeper can wait."

Connects to → Everything downstream. If mission and mandate aren't clear, the rest of the company drifts.

Core

The Four TA Selection Criteria

When evaluating whether a therapeutic area is worth entering — or staying in — four dimensions dominate. (1) Unmet need: how many patients are suffering without good options? (2) Market size: how much money could a successful drug earn? (3) Science maturity: do we understand the biology well enough to act? (4) Competitive density: how crowded is the race already?

Evaluating whether to enter a new competition: How much prestige does it carry (unmet need)? What's the prize money (market size)? Do we have the tactical know-how (science)? How good are the other clubs entering (competition)?

Connects to → These four dimensions reappear as the valuation inputs in every downstream day.

Advanced

Breadth vs. Depth (and the Exit Question)

A diversified company plays in many TAs shallowly. A specialty company plays in one or two TAs very deeply. Neither is inherently better — but both must answer: when do we exit a TA? Exit signals include sustained underperformance, science evolving away from your platform, or a mandate change. TA exit discipline is one of the hardest capabilities to build: portfolios naturally accumulate history, not intent.

A club that competes in domestic league, FA Cup, League Cup, Europa League, friendlies abroad, and women's football is broad. A club focused exclusively on top-flight league football is deep. Exiting a competition ("we won't prioritize the FA Cup anymore") is politically painful but sometimes necessary.

Connects to → Day 4 divestiture decisions; Day 5 reading when rivals exit a TA is a massive signal.

Mentor's Signature Analogy — Day 2

Every TA decision has the same shape as this question: "Should our club enter the Women's Super League?" It is not a yes/no — it is a portfolio-fit question. You look at your mission (are you a family club or a men's-only brand?), the state of the league (prestige, prize money, maturity), your internal capabilities (do you even have a women's academy?), and what your rivals are doing. The answer is almost never "because the league exists." The answer is "because entering it, at this scale, aligns with who we are becoming."

Therapeutic areas are leagues. And the quiet test of a great strategy team is not the leagues they enter — it is the leagues they refuse to enter, calmly, with a crisp reason.

Checkpoint

"A company with a 'diversified' archetype and a mission of 'broad patient access' is considering entering a rare-disease TA where the science is strong but total patients number only a few thousand worldwide. What two archetype-level tensions does this create, and what question should strategy be asking before the four TA criteria even get scored?"

The question is about archetype alignment, not numbers. Look for the mismatch between "broad access" and "few thousand patients."
Feynman Teach-Back

In three sentences, explain to someone outside pharma why a big company might refuse to develop a drug that solves a real unmet medical need.

▶ tap to reveal a framework answer
Framework Answer

"You know how a big family restaurant might refuse to start serving sushi, even though people want it? It's not that sushi is bad — it's that they don't have sushi chefs, their suppliers don't stock raw fish, and their brand is about comfort food. A pharma company can say no to a real unmet need if the science doesn't fit their platform, the market is too small for their cost base, or it would distract from the leagues they've committed to. Memory Hook

The four TA criteria form the mnemonic U · M · S · C — "Unmet, Market, Science, Competition." Picture a bow tied by four ribbons pulling in four directions: each one has to be strong enough to hold, or the bow slips.

III

Day 3 · Portfolio Management & Capital Allocation

~4 hours Bloom's: Apply → Analyze Core
+
Curiosity Hook

Of every 100 drug candidates that enter Phase 1 human trials, only about 9 to 12 reach FDA approval. A working portfolio model has to budget for the 88 that will fail — and still deliver a return.

What you'll be able to do

  • Explain the stage-gate system and why pharma is run as a sequence of explicit go/no-go decisions.
  • Interpret Probability of Success (PoS) benchmarks and explain why they matter more than gross sales forecasts.
  • Decompose the rNPV formula (risk-adjusted Net Present Value) and name what each term represents.
  • Explain the intuition behind real options — why "the option to wait" is itself worth money.

Key Concepts

Foundation

Pipeline Stages & Stage-Gate Governance

A pharma pipeline is not a conveyor belt. It is a series of gates. At each gate (end of Discovery, end of Preclinical, end of Phase 1, end of Phase 2, end of Phase 3), a governance committee asks: go, stop, or modify? The right to continue is earned, not assumed. The stage-gate system exists precisely to make killing a program a normal, sanctioned event rather than a failure.

A football academy is a stage-gate system. At U-12, U-15, U-18, Reserves, you decide: promote, release, or loan. The manager isn't allowed to refuse to release a player "because he tried hard" — the governance exists to make the hard call routine.

Connects to → Every downstream valuation multiplies outcomes by the probability of passing the next gate.

Core

Probability of Success (PoS)

PoS is the empirical probability that an asset at a given phase will eventually reach approval. Industry benchmarks (from BioMedTracker, IQVIA, and similar) show the ruthless attrition: entering Phase 1 ≈ 10% chance of approval. Entering Phase 2 ≈ 15–20%. Entering Phase 3 ≈ 50–60%. PoS varies enormously by therapeutic area — oncology PoS is far lower than anti-infectives, for example.

An academy striker's probability of ever playing first-team Premier League football. At U-15 it's maybe 2%. By Reserves it's 20%. After a pre-season goal against a senior side, perhaps 50%. The bar moves up only when evidence accumulates.

Connects to → The first input into every valuation. Without PoS, rNPV is just NPV — and useless.

Advanced

rNPV — Risk-Adjusted Net Present Value

The single most common valuation currency in pharma strategy. In plain English: every future cashflow an asset might generate, multiplied by the probability it actually happens, then discounted back to today's dollars. Three moving parts: (1) forecast of peak sales and their trajectory, (2) the probability chain of passing every remaining gate, (3) a discount rate reflecting time value and risk. Change any of the three, the number changes. Strategy debates are really debates about these three inputs.

Valuing a 17-year-old academy player today. You model his possible salaries if he makes it, multiply by the probability he actually does (not high), and discount because those salaries would be years away. One player's rNPV is much lower than his potential peak earnings — and that's the whole point.

Connects to → Day 4's deal valuation is rNPV applied to an asset you are considering buying.

Advanced

Real Options — The Value of Waiting

Classical NPV treats a decision as now-or-never. Real options thinking rejects that. It says: you can wait until uncertainty resolves before committing more capital, and that flexibility itself has monetary value. In pharma, this is how you explain why killing a program after a disappointing Phase 2 readout is rational, even if the forecast still looks positive — because the optionality you preserve by stopping is worth more than the upside you give up.

Deciding whether to trigger a £20m buy-back clause on a loan player. You could decide today. Or you could watch him play ten more matches — your option to decide later is worth money, because you'll know more. Forcing a decision prematurely throws that option value away.

Connects to → Day 4's option deals and Day 5's scenario monitoring — both are real-options thinking made practical.

Mentor's Signature Analogy — Day 3

Imagine you're the director of football managing an academy of 150 kids. You know — statistically — that only one or two will ever be regular first-team players. Your job is not to predict which one. Your job is to run a system that sorts, promotes, kills, and reallocates across the 150 fairly and repeatedly, so that the one or two who emerge genuinely deserve it. If you fall in love with a particular kid and refuse to release him, you starve the others of coaching time. That's portfolio management.

The rNPV number is just the dispassionate way of putting that logic on a spreadsheet: multiply what you might gain by the probability you'll actually gain it, and now you can compare Kid A to Kid B without sentiment.

Checkpoint

"Asset A has a forecast peak sales of $4bn and is in Phase 1. Asset B has forecast peak sales of $900m and is in Phase 3. Without doing the full math, which is likely to have the higher rNPV, and why might the answer be counter-intuitive to someone outside pharma?"

Do the rough PoS multiplication in your head: 4bn × ~10% vs. 900m × ~60%. Also think about time-to-cash and discounting.
Feynman Teach-Back

Explain to a 12-year-old why a pharmaceutical company might deliberately kill a drug program that still looks like it could work.

▶ tap to reveal a framework answer
Framework Answer

"You know how, in a school bake sale, you only have one oven and you have to pick which cake to bake next? If your chocolate cake is okay but your lemon cake is probably going to sell much more, you stop baking the chocolate one — even though it might have been fine — because the oven time is worth more on the lemon. Pharma companies kill drug programs the same way: not because the drug is bad, but because the people, money, and factories are worth more on a different program. It's not about the drug being a failure — it's about whether it's the best use of the oven."

Memory Hook

Remember the rNPV formula as CASH × CHANCE ÷ TIME — the three questions in every portfolio debate: how much cash could this generate, what chance does it have of happening, and how far in the future? Fix one of the three and you've fixed the argument.

IV

Day 4 · BD&L — The Art of the Deal

~4 hours Bloom's: Analyze → Evaluate Stretch
+
Curiosity Hook

For many of the world's top-selling drugs, the company selling them is not the company that discovered them. More than half of big-pharma revenue today comes from assets that were bought, licensed, or partnered — not invented in-house.

What you'll be able to do

  • Place any deal on the commitment spectrum, from low-commitment options to full acquisition.
  • Name the six layers of the due diligence stack and what each layer is trying to falsify.
  • Explain why deals are valued on scenario rNPV vs. price paid, not absolute value.
  • Recognize complex structures — CVRs, milestones, earn-outs — and why they exist.

Key Concepts

Foundation

The Deal-Type Spectrum

Deals sit on a continuum of commitment. At the low-commitment end: an option deal (pay a small fee now to have the right to buy later). Then: in-licensing (rights to develop and sell, with royalties back). Then: co-promotion or JV (shared rights, shared economics). At the high-commitment end: outright acquisition (you own it, you run it). The further right you go, the more control and upside you get — and the more capital and risk you commit.

The transfer market is the same spectrum: short loan → loan with option to buy → permanent transfer → whole-club acquisition. Each step up is more money, more control, less reversibility.

Connects to → Day 2 archetype choice drives which end of the spectrum a company prefers.

Core

The Due Diligence Stack

Before a deal closes, six layers of due diligence run in parallel. Scientific: is the biology real? Clinical: do the trial data hold up? CMC (chemistry, manufacturing, controls): can it actually be made at scale and to quality? IP: is the patent estate defensible? Commercial: will payers pay and prescribers prescribe? Regulatory: will the FDA/EMA approve the label you're modeling? Each layer can kill the deal alone. Strong deals require all six to hold.

Signing a big-money player: medical exam (clinical), video review (scientific), fitness test (CMC), agent negotiation (commercial), work-permit check (regulatory), and current-contract clearance (IP). Any one failing scrubs the deal.

Connects to → Day 5 CI teams often produce the commercial and competitive inputs into DD.

Core

Scenario rNPV vs. Price Paid

A deal is never valued as a single number. It is valued as a range: bear, base, bull rNPV — each with different assumptions about PoS, peak sales, and time to launch. A disciplined deal team asks three questions: (1) Is the base-case rNPV above the price? (2) Does the bear case still break even? (3) Is the bull case large enough to justify strategic risk? A deal that only works in the bull case is usually a warning sign.

A transfer fee of £80m for a 22-year-old. Base case: he becomes a top-six striker (justifies £80m). Bear: he's injured three seasons out of five (does it still break even?). Bull: he becomes the best in the league (does it beat the next-best use of the £80m?).

Connects to → Day 3 rNPV machinery is the engine; Day 4 adds the negotiation overlay.

Advanced

Complex Structures (CVRs, Milestones, Earn-Outs)

When buyer and seller disagree about the future, they can split the disagreement structurally. Milestone payments: extra money flows only if specific future events happen (Phase 3 success, FDA approval, first commercial sale). CVRs (contingent value rights): shares that only pay out if future conditions are met. Earn-outs: additional consideration based on future performance. These instruments buy time, letting the price adjust as uncertainty resolves — and they are textbook real-options thinking applied to deal structure.

Bonus clauses in a player's contract: £5m extra if he plays 30 games, £10m if the team qualifies for Champions League, £20m if he wins Player of the Year. The headline fee is smaller; the total value tracks the future.

Connects to → Directly implements the "value of waiting" from Day 3's real options.

Figure 4.1 — The deal-type spectrum: from optionality to ownership
Mentor's Signature Analogy — Day 4

A due diligence committee is essentially a pre-match briefing. Six specialists in a room, each one assigned to find the weakness no one else will spot: the scientist asks "does the biology actually work?", the clinician asks "did the patients really improve or is the trial noisy?", the manufacturing engineer asks "can we make this at scale or will every batch drift?", the lawyer asks "does someone else's patent sit on top of ours?", the commercial lead asks "will doctors actually prescribe and payers actually pay?", and the regulator-whisperer asks "what will the FDA label actually say?"

A bad deal team agrees. A great deal team argues. Each specialist's job is to try and kill the deal. If, after all six have genuinely tried, the deal is still standing — you have something worth signing.

Checkpoint

"A biotech is offering you a Phase 2 rare-disease asset. They want $2bn upfront. Your team models base-case rNPV at $2.5bn, bear case at $0.8bn, bull case at $6bn. Why might you still walk away, and how might an alternative deal structure rescue the transaction?"

Walk-away reason: bear case is below the cheque. Rescue: shift some of that $2bn from "upfront" to "milestone" — that's CVR / earn-out logic.
Feynman Teach-Back

Explain to a 12-year-old why a pharma company would ever choose a complicated deal with milestones, rather than just paying a single, simpler price.

▶ tap to reveal a framework answer
Framework Answer

"You know how, when you trade cards with a friend, sometimes you can't agree on who has the better card? One way to fix it is to say: 'I'll give you my card now, but if my card turns out to be super rare, you owe me another one later.' Pharma deals work the same way. The seller thinks their drug will be amazing; the buyer isn't sure yet. Instead of fighting about the price today, they agree: pay a smaller amount now, and pay more later if the drug hits certain milestones. Both sides can walk away feeling fair — and the buyer doesn't have to bet on a future nobody can yet see."

Memory Hook

The six DD layers spell S · C · C · I · C · R — Scientific, Clinical, CMC, IP, Commercial, Regulatory. Or, more usefully: remember the image of a six-legged stool holding up a deal. Kick any one leg, the deal falls.

V

Day 5 · Competitive Intelligence & Integration

~3.5 hours Bloom's: Evaluate → Create Stretch
+
Curiosity Hook

Most meaningful competitive surprises in pharma were visible in public data months before they hit the news — in a conference abstract, a patent filing, or a job advert for an unusual new capability. Competitive intelligence is less about secrets than about paying attention.

What you'll be able to do

  • Name the main public sources competitive intelligence draws on and why they matter.
  • Explain the annual rhythm of CI — major congresses (ASCO, AHA, EHA) as synchronization points.
  • Describe war-gaming and why it is a strategic tool, not a prediction tool.
  • Integrate all four pillars into one end-to-end strategic loop.

Key Concepts

Foundation

CI Sources — The Public Footprint

Serious CI is built on public, legal sources. Clinical trial registries (ClinicalTrials.gov) reveal what's being tested, where, and how. Conference abstracts reveal results months before peer-reviewed publication. Patent filings reveal platform intentions. Company earnings calls reveal strategic priorities. Job postings reveal capability investments. SEC filings reveal deal rumors and risk factors. A CI analyst is a trained reader — spotting signal inside public noise.

A scout watching an opposing club isn't stealing secrets — she's watching every match, reading every press conference, noting who's missing from training, and counting youth-team appearances. The patterns tell the story.

Connects to → These same sources feed Day 2 TA scouting and Day 4 deal sourcing.

Core

The Congress Calendar

Certain scientific congresses act as synchronized clocks for entire therapeutic areas. In oncology, ASCO (June) and ESMO (September–October) are when major readouts happen. In cardiology, AHA (November) and ACC (March/April). In hematology, EHA (June) and ASH (December). CI teams plan the year around these dates: pre-conference anticipation, abstract-release briefings, live-at-conference reporting, and post-conference synthesis. Strategy debates move on congress rhythms as much as fiscal-quarter rhythms.

Transfer windows (January and summer) and fixture congestion (Christmas) are the football equivalent: the rhythm of the year is fixed, and everything else plans around those moments.

Connects to → Day 2 mandate reviews are often timed to major-congress readouts.

Advanced

War-Gaming

A war-game is a structured exercise in which internal teams play the part of competitors. Team A is your company; Team B is Pfizer; Team C is Merck. Each team gets the same public data, makes strategic moves (launch decisions, pricing, trial designs), and sees the consequences. The goal is not to predict the future — it is to pressure-test your own strategy by forcing yourself to see the board from outside. The best output of a war-game is usually a surprise your own team did not consider.

Top football managers do this constantly: "If I were the Atlético manager preparing for our match, what would I see as our weakness?" Building the opponent's game plan is how you find the holes in your own.

Connects to → Day 4 deal strategy and Day 3 portfolio prioritization both improve after a war-game stress-tests them.

Advanced

The Integrated Strategic Loop

The four pillars are not separate. They form a loop that runs continuously. TA strategy sets the frame → portfolio management lives within that frame → BD&L fills gaps the portfolio reveals → CI monitors whether the frame is still valid. If CI spots an environmental change (a rival's breakthrough, a policy shift, a disruptive modality), the loop restarts from TA strategy. The great strategy teams run this loop fast: new information flows from CI back into TA mandates within weeks, not years.

A club's football brain works the same way: league ambition (TA) shapes the squad (portfolio), the squad reveals gaps to fill in the market (BD&L), and scouting (CI) tells you whether your rivals just changed the rules.

Mentor's Signature Analogy — Day 5

Think of CI as the peripheral vision of the strategy team. The other three pillars look forward, at the drug, the portfolio, the deal. CI looks sideways — at everything that isn't your own plan. Without peripheral vision, even the best plan is walked into by a pedestrian you never saw coming.

But here's the real insight: CI is only as valuable as the speed with which it reshapes the other three pillars. A CI team that produces beautiful reports nobody reads is worthless. A CI team whose intel changed a TA mandate last month, a portfolio decision last week, and a deal term yesterday — that team is the nervous system of the company.

Checkpoint

"Your CI team reports that a major rival has quietly posted job adverts for 30 gene-therapy scientists in a therapeutic area where your company's current portfolio is all small molecules. Walk through, in one sentence each, how this single piece of intelligence should ripple through pillars ①, ②, and ③ — TA strategy, portfolio, and BD&L."

① Question whether your TA mandate still holds if the field is moving to a new modality. ② Ask whether your small-molecule assets are now structurally disadvantaged. ③ Ask whether there's a gene-therapy biotech you should now be scouting.

The four pillars as one loop: ① FIELDS · ② FORCES · ③ FILLS · ④ FOES. Fields you fight on (TA). Forces you already have (Portfolio). Fills the gaps (BD&L). Foes change everything (CI). Say it out loud three times; it sticks.

Pitfalls & Misconceptions

The five traps that catch almost every beginner.

i.

"The big NPV number wins."

Myth: Whichever asset has the highest forecast sales is the best asset.

Reality: Forecast without probability is fiction. A $5bn asset at 10% PoS is, in expected value, worse than a $1bn asset at 70% PoS. This is why the r in rNPV matters as much as the NPV.

Matters on Day 3.

ii.

"BD&L is the glamorous part."

Myth: Deals are where strategy really happens.

Reality: Deals are executions of a strategy set upstream. The hardest work — choosing the TAs, setting the mandate, sizing the portfolio envelope — happens before any deal team is involved. A bad TA strategy cannot be rescued by a brilliant deal.

Matters on Days 2 and 4.

iii.

"CI predicts the future."

Myth: The job of competitive intelligence is to tell you what rivals will do.

Reality: CI doesn't predict — it pressure-tests. Nobody reliably predicts rival behavior; the useful product is a set of plausible scenarios that force your own strategy to be robust across all of them. Prediction invites overconfidence; scenarios invite readiness.

Matters on Day 5.

iv.

"Killing programs is failure."

Myth: A successful portfolio team shouldn't be killing many programs.

Reality: The opposite. A healthy stage-gate system must kill most programs — because biology kills most programs, and governance exists to make that killing timely rather than painful. A team that never kills is a team that doesn't believe its own data.

Matters on Day 3.

v.

"The four pillars are separate departments."

Myth: TA strategy, portfolio, BD&L, and CI each live in their own corner.

Reality: They are one loop. An insight from CI re-opens TA mandate. A gap in the portfolio spawns a BD&L search. A deal shifts the portfolio which shifts TA allocation. Treating them as separate departments produces a strategy that looks coherent on paper and fails in practice.

Matters everywhere — especially Day 5.

vi.

The meta-trap — "I should understand it all perfectly before moving on."

Myth: Each concept must be fully mastered before the next one is attempted.

Reality: Strategy concepts interlock — rNPV only fully makes sense once you've seen it applied in both portfolio (Day 3) and a deal (Day 4). Make peace with temporary fuzziness; it resolves on the second pass through the material.

Cross-Domain Connections

The same four pillars, visible in three very different worlds.

The strategic loop (Fields → Forces → Fills → Foes) isn't a pharma invention. It re-appears, under different vocabulary, in any field that runs long-horizon bets under uncertainty.

Venture Capital

Thesis → Portfolio → Deal Flow → Market Intel

A VC firm's investment thesis is its TA strategy. Its portfolio of startups is the pharma pipeline. New investments are BD&L deals. Market intelligence on emerging sectors is CI. Even the rNPV logic translates — VCs routinely price companies as probability-weighted exit scenarios.

Film Studios

Slate → Greenlights → Acquisitions → Rival Watch

A studio decides which genres and franchises to invest in (TA strategy). Its slate of films at various stages (script, production, post, release) is the portfolio. Buying indie IP or distribution rights is BD&L. Tracking competitor release calendars is CI. The stage-gate logic is identical: projects get killed at every step.

Military & Defense

Doctrine → Force Structure → Procurement → Intel

A nation sets its strategic doctrine (TA). Its force structure — branches, units, capabilities — is the portfolio. Weapons procurement and alliance-building is BD&L. Intelligence services are CI. War-gaming, in fact, is borrowed directly from this domain into pharma strategy.

These three parallels mean your brain already has multiple pathways to recall the four pillars. Any time a pharma concept feels foreign, translate it into one of these three worlds first — then translate back.

Mentor's Parting Wisdom

A closing note — on learning a field made of answers to four questions.

Strategy & BD&L can look, from the outside, like an intimidating wall of acronyms and valuation spreadsheets. It isn't. Underneath all of it is a very small number of recurring questions — the four pillars — that every pharmaceutical company on earth is asking, every day. Once you can hear those four questions underneath any meeting or any slide deck, the field stops feeling foreign. You've already learned them.

The confusion you feel in the first pass of this material is not a sign that you're behind. It is a sign that you're encountering a domain where concepts genuinely interlock — where rNPV on Day 3 only fully makes sense after you've seen it applied to deal structure on Day 4, and where CI on Day 5 only really clicks once you've already understood the TA mandate from Day 2. Don't fight the temporary fuzziness. Just come back for a second pass. The second pass is where the learning compounds.

The insider secret nobody states out loud: strategy in pharma is mostly a disciplined practice of saying no. No to the TA that doesn't fit the archetype. No to the program that stopped making sense at Phase 2. No to the deal that only works in the bull case. The art isn't in the yes — it's in the clean, reasoned, unsentimental no. If you can hold that one idea, the rest of Module 1 falls into place.

— Hold the map. The territory is learnable. You've already started.
