Module 11 · Market Access · Ten-Day Deep Dive

The Watershed.

Every dollar of US drug revenue flows through an engineered hydraulic system — locks, sluice gates, branching channels, federal dams, and quiet evaporation along the route. Market Access is the discipline of mapping that watershed, knowing where the volume is lost, and engineering the flow that survives.

The hook

On August 15, 2024, CMS announced something the United States had never produced before: negotiated prices for prescription drugs paid by Medicare. The first ten — Eliquis, Jardiance, Xarelto, Januvia, Farxiga, Entresto, Enbrel, Imbruvica, Stelara, and the NovoLog/Fiasp insulins — accounted for roughly $50 billion in Part D spending. The Maximum Fair Prices were 38% to 79% below list, with a weighted average reduction near 60%. The MFPs take effect January 1, 2026. Fifteen more drugs were named in early 2025. Fifteen more in 2026. Then twenty more annually. The Inflation Reduction Act has fundamentally rewired how money flows through the US drug system, and every market-access strategy written before August 2022 needs to be re-engineered against a federal pricing floor that did not exist before — a floor that propagates, through a tangle of Medicaid best-price rules and commercial reference behaviour, into nearly every contract a manufacturer holds. The watershed has a new dam. The river runs differently downstream. Anyone working in pharma access who does not understand the new flow is, at best, navigating from an obsolete map.

The elevator pitch

Market Access is the engineered watershed of US drug payment.

A watershed is a complete drainage system: from springs at the headwaters, through a network of locks, sluice gates, branching tributaries, side channels, dams, and tolls, to a delta at the sea. Engineers who manage watersheds know that the volume at the source is rarely the volume at the mouth — water is taxed at every lock, redirected through preferred channels, lost to evaporation, diverted into special-rate canals, and sometimes blocked entirely by structures upstream. Their job is not to wish the watershed simpler. Their job is to know exactly how it works, where the losses happen, and what engineering choices control the flow that ultimately reaches the sea.

The US drug payment system is a watershed of nearly identical structure, and the analogy is not loose — the institutional machinery has the same topology. The manufacturer is the headwaters; the wholesale acquisition cost is the peak discharge volume. The first lock is the wholesaler-distributor margin. The next set of locks belongs to the pharmacy benefit managers — the lock-keepers of the modern US drug system, charging tolls (rebates, administrative fees) for the right of passage through their formulary networks. The sluice gates are formulary tiers and utilization-management mechanisms (PA, ST, QL); they decide which water reaches which downstream basin. The river branches into three principal channels — retail pharmacy, specialty pharmacy, buy-and-bill — each with its own engineering. There is a contraband waterway called 340B that operates under different rules and quietly carries massive volume. There is now a federal dam called the IRA, with a maximum water level called the Maximum Fair Price. There is a backflow tax called inflation rebates. There is a downstream maximum-draft limit called the catastrophic cap. And at the delta, a small fraction of the original headwaters volume actually reaches patients as filled prescriptions — the rest having been collected, diverted, or evaporated along the route.

The master analogy — the engineered watershed

Picture an aerial map of a major river system that has been engineered for centuries — locks every few miles, sluice gates regulating flow, branching canals serving different cities, side channels for special trade, federal dams controlling peak discharge, toll-collection points at every meaningful junction. The river-system engineer reads this map not as a complication but as a known operating environment. Every lock has an operator with documented rules. Every toll has a published rate. Every sluice gate has a position determined by upstream agreements. The engineering knowledge is what lets cargo move efficiently from source to market; the absence of that knowledge is what produces failures, delays, lost volume, and stranded shipments.

Market Access is exactly this discipline, applied to the US pharmaceutical revenue system. The watershed is the entire payment topology from manufacturer to patient. The headwaters is the manufacturer's revenue source. WAC (wholesale acquisition cost) is the peak discharge volume — the published gross price before any deduction. The lock-keepers are PBMs — Express Scripts, CVS Caremark, OptumRx, plus their increasingly central rebate aggregators (Zinc, Ascent, Emisar). Formulary tiers are the sluice gates that determine which prescriptions flow at preferred rates and which are blocked or restricted. Utilization management tools (prior authorization, step therapy, quantity limits) are the manual sluice mechanisms operated by lock-tenders. Rebates and chargebacks are the tolls collected at each lock. Channels — retail, specialty, buy-and-bill — are the river branches, each with distinct hydraulics. 340B is the contraband waterway, operating under a 1992 statutory exemption that has grown into a parallel pricing system carrying tens of billions in volume. The IRA is the federal dam constructed in 2022, controlling peak discharge from January 1, 2026 onward. The MFP is the maximum water level. Inflation rebates are a backflow tax — penalizing the manufacturer when WAC rises faster than CPI-U. The catastrophic cap ($2,000 OOP, effective 2025) is the maximum draft limit at the delta — patients can no longer be required to wade deeper than this. Outcomes-based agreements are performance-based tolls — adjusting upward or downward based on whether the cargo arrived as promised. Hub services and copay programs are the lock-tender services that help patient-vessels actually navigate the system. And the net price is what reaches the sea — typically 40-65% of headwaters volume in branded drugs, sometimes much less.

Origin story

How the US drug watershed was engineered — three regulatory inflections and one missed reform.

For most of the 20th century, US drug pricing was a private negotiation between manufacturers and a relatively undifferentiated insurance market. The transformation into the engineered watershed we now navigate happened in three distinct waves, each layered on top of the prior, none of them ever cleanly displacing what came before.

Wave one (1965–1990): the public payers. The 1965 establishment of Medicare and Medicaid created the first large public buyers of US healthcare, but neither initially had aggressive drug-pricing authority. The 1990 Medicaid Drug Rebate Program (OBRA '90) established the first statutory rebate — manufacturers must pay Medicaid the difference between AMP and either 23.1% of AMP or the best price they offer any other commercial purchaser ("Medicaid best price"). This single provision became one of the most consequential pricing rules in US healthcare, because it created an implicit floor: any price offered to a commercial payer below the Medicaid best-price calculation gets matched in Medicaid rebates, multiplying the cost of the discount across the entire Medicaid book. Best price has been the silent governor on commercial contracting ever since.

Wave two (1992–2010): the parallel channels. The 1992 Veterans Health Care Act established the 340B Drug Pricing Program, originally intended to support a small set of safety-net hospitals serving low-income populations. Through subsequent statutory expansions (DSH eligibility, contract pharmacy guidance from 2010), 340B grew from a niche program into a parallel pricing system now covering tens of billions in annual drug purchases. The 1996 Express Scripts IPO and the rise of the modern PBM consolidated retail pharmacy management; the 2003 Medicare Modernization Act created Part D and the buy-and-bill economics of Part B (ASP+6%, later eroded by sequestration to ~4.3%). The 2010 Affordable Care Act expanded Medicaid, formalized 340B contract pharmacy, and introduced the Sunshine Act for industry-physician transactions — but did not directly negotiate Medicare drug prices, an omission that became the political pressure point for the next decade.

Wave three (2010–2022): the integrated PBMs and the rebate economy. The 2010s witnessed massive consolidation among PBMs and their vertical integration with insurers and specialty pharmacies — Express Scripts merged with Cigna (2018), CVS Health acquired Aetna (2018), UnitedHealth's OptumRx integrated with UnitedHealthcare. The "Big Three" PBMs came to administer drug benefits for roughly 80% of US covered lives. In parallel, the rebate aggregator emerged — Zinc Health Services (Express Scripts/Cigna), Ascent Health Services (Cigna/Express Scripts/Prime), and Emisar Pharma Services (Optum) — entities domiciled offshore that collect rebates on behalf of multiple PBM clients, creating an additional opaque layer between manufacturer and ultimate payer. The gross-to-net bubble — the ratio of WAC to net price — grew dramatically across most therapeutic areas, with branded retail drugs commonly losing 50-70% of WAC to rebates and discounts before reaching the manufacturer's books.

The fourth wave (2022–present): federal price-setting. The Inflation Reduction Act (signed August 16, 2022) ended a fifty-year bipartisan consensus that Medicare would not directly negotiate drug prices. The IRA introduced (i) the Drug Price Negotiation Program — Medicare-negotiated MFPs for selected high-spend Part D and (eventually) Part B drugs, (ii) Part D inflation rebates with a CPI-U benchmark, (iii) Part D redesign — including the $2,000 OOP cap, eliminated coverage gap, and a manufacturer discount in the catastrophic phase, (iv) insulin OOP caps. The first ten negotiated drugs (announced August 2023) received their MFPs on August 15, 2024, with discounts ranging 38-79% off list price. The MFPs take effect January 1, 2026. Fifteen more drugs entered the second negotiation cycle (announced January 2025, MFPs effective 2027). The cadence then shifts: fifteen more for 2028, twenty per year thereafter — with Part B drugs entering the program from 2028 onward.

The watershed you must now operate in has all four waves layered on top of each other. The Medicaid best-price floor from 1990. The 340B parallel channel from 1992. The PBM-aggregator complex from 2010s. And the IRA federal dam from 2022. None of these systems have ever been cleanly redesigned; they have been added, one on top of the other, producing a hydraulic empire whose total complexity is now beyond what any single human can fully model. The market access professional's job is to navigate it anyway.

The terrain — who pays whom for what

+

Before you can model coverage, contract for access, or design a launch, you must internalize the basic fact that "the US drug payer" is not one entity but at least seven distinct populations operating under five distinct legal regimes — each with its own pricing rules, its own coverage architecture, and its own political constituencies. Most failures in market access begin with someone treating these as a single market.

  • Understand what each segment pays, how it covers drugs, and which manufacturer revenue stream it touches

The four key concepts

The seven US payer segments

(1) Commercial — employer-sponsored insurance (ESI) and individual marketplace, ~180M lives, administered through PBMs, governed by ERISA. (2) Medicare Part B — physician-administered drugs, ~65M eligible, paid at ASP+4.3% (post-sequestration) under buy-and-bill. (3) Medicare Part D — outpatient retail/specialty drugs, ~50M enrolled in stand-alone PDPs and MA-PD plans. (4) Medicaid — ~85M, fee-for-service in some states, MCO-managed in most, governed by Medicaid Drug Rebate Program. (5) 340B — discount drug program for safety-net entities, $50B+ in 2023 purchases. (6) VA/DoD — federal supply schedule pricing under Federal Ceiling Price (FCP). (7) Cash — uninsured plus high-deductible patients pre-deductible.

Seven distinct river systems sharing the same continental watershed but governed by different jurisdictional regimes. The molecule that flows easily through one may be blocked, taxed, or bypassed entirely in another.

The four distribution channels

(1) Retail pharmacy — Walgreens, CVS, independents — most oral solid drugs, dispensed at the local pharmacy, billed through pharmacy benefit (Part D / commercial Rx). (2) Specialty pharmacy (SP) — limited-distribution networks (typically 5-10 SPs per drug), high-cost specialty oral and self-injectable, dispensed via mail with clinical wraparound services; the "Big Three" SPs (Accredo, CVS Specialty, Optum Specialty) plus boutique SPs. (3) Buy-and-bill — physician offices and hospital outpatient departments purchase the drug, administer it, then bill the medical benefit (Part B / commercial medical); applies to most physician-administered injectables and infusions. (4) Mail-order — typically retail equivalents at 90-day fills, often PBM-owned.

Four river branches, each with distinct hydraulics. The molecule's molecular form (oral vs. injectable), administration setting (self vs. clinical), and cost profile (specialty vs. retail) determine which channel it flows through. Some drugs flow through one; some through several.

The "access" cascade — what coverage really means

"Coverage" is not access. The funnel from "covered" to "actually filled and paid" runs through five steps: (1) Lives covered — the plan has the drug somewhere on its formulary. (2) Formulary tier — preferred / non-preferred / specialty / not covered. (3) UM constraints — prior authorization, step therapy, quantity limits, age/diagnosis edits. (4) PA outcome — approved, denied, approved with restriction. (5) Paid claim — drug actually dispensed and reimbursed at the agreed rate. Industry leakage between covered and paid-claim is routinely 30-60%; understanding where in the funnel the loss happens is the central diagnostic of access analytics.

A boat at the headwaters being "covered" only means it has been told the river is open. Whether it actually reaches the sea depends on how many locks it passes, how many sluice gates open in time, and whether any toll is uncollectible. Coverage is the start of the journey, not the journey.

The post-IRA policy vocabulary

Six terms that did not matter in 2021 and dominate every conversation now. IRA — Inflation Reduction Act, signed August 16, 2022. MFP — Maximum Fair Price, the negotiated price for selected drugs. Selected drug — high-spend Part D drug (initial cycles) chosen by CMS for negotiation. Inflation rebate — manufacturer rebate to Medicare when drug-price growth exceeds CPI-U, applies to both Part B and Part D. Part D redesign — 2025 restructure of the Part D benefit: $2,000 OOP cap, eliminated coverage gap, manufacturer discount in catastrophic phase. Manufacturer discount program — replaces the Coverage Gap Discount Program; manufacturers now pay 10% in initial coverage and 20% in catastrophic phase for applicable drugs.

The new federal hydraulic engineering vocabulary. Until 2022 the river ran on its existing locks and tolls; now there is a federal dam being commissioned, with new water-level rules, new backflow taxes, and a new manufacturer-paid downstream subsidy that fundamentally changes who pays what.

Mentor's note

The most important diagnostic discipline I have ever learned in market access is to refuse to talk about coverage as a single number. When a brand team says "we have 80% commercial coverage," I always ask the same six follow-up questions, in sequence: Eighty percent of which lives — total commercial, or just our addressable indication? At what tier — preferred, non-preferred, or specialty? With what UM — open access, prior auth required, step therapy, quantity limits? With what PA approval rate — 90%, 60%, 30%? With what time-to-fill — 3 days, 14 days, 45 days? With what first-fill abandonment — 5%, 25%, 55%? Five of those six answers are usually unknown when the original "80% covered" number is reported. The discrepancy between covered and paid-claim is, in most specialty launches, the single largest unmodeled commercial risk — bigger than any pricing question, any clinical risk, or any competitive entry. Internalize the cascade as the unit of analysis, not the headline number, and you will find yourself diagnosing problems six months earlier than the brand team that is still operating from the dashboard.

Your specialty oral oncology drug has commercial coverage on 92% of lives, Part D coverage on 88% of lives, but only 41% paid-claim rate among prescribed patients in real-world data. Where in the cascade is the leakage, and what data would you pull to diagnose it?

One articulation

The 92%/88% headline is a coverage-tier statement; the 41% is a paid-claim outcome. The gap of ~50 percentage points is almost certainly distributed across UM friction, PA denial/abandonment, and patient OOP-driven first-fill abandonment, with proportions varying by segment. Diagnostic data: (1) Formulary tier distribution from MMIT/DRG by plan and segment — if much of the "covered" volume is on non-preferred or specialty tiers with high coinsurance, that is the upstream OOP problem. (2) UM constraints across the covered book — what % requires PA, ST, QL; map to plan share. (3) Claims-level rejection data from APLD/IQVIA — segment by rejection reason and recovery (was the rejected claim ever paid for the same patient?). (4) Time-to-fill from hub data — days from Rx to first paid claim, segmented by plan; long tails (>30 days) typically indicate PA-resolution friction. (5) Hub funnel — patients enrolled, BV completed, BI completed, PA submitted, PA approved, dispensed, fill-1, fill-2; identify the largest drop-off station. (6) Patient OOP at first fill — distribution segmented by plan; abandonment-vs-OOP curve. Likely diagnosis pattern: in oncology specialty oral, the most common 50-point leakage decomposes into roughly 15-20 points UM friction (PA delays beyond 14 days), 10-15 points PA denial/abandonment, 10-15 points first-fill abandonment driven by Part D deductible-phase OOP (the $2K cap helps but does not eliminate the deductible problem), and 5-10 points "physician switched to alternative therapy mid-process" recorded as no fill. Intervention prioritization is diagnosis-dependent: if UM is the lead loss, the lever is hub-supported PA navigation + payer-criteria optimization. If OOP is the lead loss, the lever is copay program design + bridge therapy + hub-supported PAP. If denial is the lead loss, the lever is upstream contract negotiation. The same 41% paid-claim rate has at least three different operational responses depending on where the loss actually occurs — and the only way to pick the right intervention is to see the cascade decomposed by station.

The lock-keepers — PBMs, formularies, and the rebate economy

+

The PBM is the single most consequential institution in US drug payment, and almost no one outside the industry can articulate accurately what one does. The mainstream account ("PBMs administer drug benefits and negotiate rebates") is true but incomplete — like saying a customs office "administers trade and collects duties." The real description involves vertical integration with insurers and specialty pharmacies, offshore rebate aggregation, the spread between revealed and unrevealed pricing, and an architecture of incentives whose net effect on US drug spending remains contested even within Congress.

The four key concepts

The Big Three PBMs & vertical integration

Three integrated entities administer drug benefits for ~80% of US covered lives. CVS Caremark (parent: CVS Health, integrated with Aetna insurance and CVS Specialty pharmacy). Express Scripts (parent: Cigna's Evernorth, integrated with Cigna insurance and Accredo specialty pharmacy). OptumRx (parent: UnitedHealth Group, integrated with UnitedHealthcare insurance and Optum Specialty pharmacy). Each owns a captive specialty pharmacy that captures most of the SP volume from its book; this captivity is one of the most consequential structural facts in modern US specialty drug economics.

Three vertically-integrated lock-keepers who also own the boats that pass through their locks, the docks the boats unload at, and the insurance policies that cover the cargo. The structural conflict of interest is not hidden; it is the operating model.

Formulary architecture

A formulary is a structured list of drugs the plan will cover, organized by tier (typical 4-6 tiers: generic / preferred brand / non-preferred brand / specialty / non-formulary; and now often a "preferred specialty" tier). Each tier has its own copay or coinsurance structure. Drugs are organized by therapeutic class; within a class, the formulary committee selects 1-2 preferred drugs (usually with the deepest rebates). Preferred drugs flow through with low patient OOP and minimal friction; non-preferred drugs face higher OOP and may face UM. Formulary placement is the central market-access lever — and the central PBM-manufacturer negotiation.

The sluice-gate map of the lock system. Some boats are flagged as priority and pass through with minimal friction; others are diverted, slowed, taxed, or blocked. The flag is set by upstream agreement and by the toll structure — and it determines almost everything about the boat's journey.

UM tools — PA, ST, QL, edits

Within formulary, utilization management tools further constrain access. Prior Authorization (PA): the prescriber must submit clinical justification before the drug is dispensed; criteria vary by plan. Step Therapy (ST): the patient must fail (or contraindicate) a designated cheaper drug before access. Quantity Limits (QL): capped daily/monthly/annual dispensing. Age/diagnosis edits: claim is rejected if patient demographics or coded diagnosis don't match payer rules. UM tools are payer-specific, frequently updated, often opaque to prescribers, and account for the majority of access friction in specialty drugs.

The manual sluice mechanisms operated by the lock-tender — wheels that must be turned, levers that must be pulled, paperwork that must be filed before the gate actually opens. Some boats have priority documentation; most don't, and discover at the lock that the wheel takes longer to turn than expected.

The rebate flow & rebate aggregators

The manufacturer sells at WAC to the wholesaler (~$X). Manufacturer pays a rebate to the PBM contingent on formulary position and market-share performance (~30-60% of WAC for branded drugs). The PBM passes some portion of the rebate to the plan sponsor (~70-90% under modern transparent contracts). The plan sponsor uses the rebate to lower its premium or, increasingly, to fund a copay-accumulator counter-strategy. Rebate aggregators — Zinc Health Services (Express Scripts/Cigna), Ascent Health Services (Cigna/Express Scripts/Prime), Emisar Pharma Services (Optum) — are PBM-owned entities, often offshore, that aggregate rebate negotiation across multiple PBM clients. They are a central feature of the post-2018 PBM landscape and a primary target of current congressional reform proposals.

The toll-collection point that has been moved offshore for tax and regulatory reasons. The water still flows through the lock; the toll is still collected; but the toll now flows through a set of intermediary accounts that are harder to inspect, and that capture economic value the original lock-keeper structure did not necessarily intend.

Mentor's note

The single most useful frame I have ever encountered for thinking about PBMs is to recognize that they are not the antagonist that drug-pricing rhetoric often makes them out to be, and they are not the neutral administrators that their own communications often claim them to be. They are commercial counterparties with structural information advantages — and the right posture toward them is the one a sophisticated B2B buyer takes toward any large counterparty: assume they are optimizing their own outcome, model their incentives precisely, expect that their stated rationale and their actual rationale may differ, and pay attention to the structural pressure points (formulary committee timing, contract renewal cadence, captive SP economics, rebate-aggregator architecture) where their incentive can be moved. The brand teams that get into adversarial relationships with PBMs lose; the brand teams that pretend the structural conflict doesn't exist also lose. The teams that win are the ones that read the PBM the way a corporate-banking customer reads JPMorgan — institutionally sophisticated, structurally complex, and worth investing in understanding rather than trying to either fight or befriend.

Feynman teach-back

A new GLP-1 entrant is launching into a class where two competitors hold preferred status with the Big Three PBMs through deep rebate contracts. What are the realistic strategic options for formulary access, and how should the team think about the access-vs-net-price trade-off?

click to reveal →
One articulation

The realistic options form a spectrum. Option 1 — Buy preferred status. Match or exceed the incumbents' rebate floors to displace one and become co-preferred. Net price compresses; volume scales rapidly. Best when (a) the molecule has a meaningful clinical advantage to defend the rebate against switching, (b) the BIM is acceptable at the lower net price, (c) the launch is a high-volume play. Option 2 — Co-preferred via differential value claim. Negotiate preferred status alongside (not displacing) one incumbent at a more modest rebate by offering a clinically-distinct value claim that lets the PBM justify wider access without cannibalizing the incumbent's deal. Net price holds higher; volume is shared. Option 3 — Non-preferred with prior auth. Accept secondary tier with mandatory PA, focus on prescribers willing to do the paperwork, deploy heavy hub support. Net price holds high; volume is constrained but margin per script is large. Often right when the molecule is genuinely differentiated for a subpopulation, the BIM at preferred pricing would be unsustainable, prescriber commitment is high. Option 4 — Limited-distribution + cash strategy. Skip broad PBM access entirely; sell through limited SP and DTC cash channels (which is what some GLP-1 entrants have actually pursued for off-label/wellness uses). The trade-off thinking: the right option is determined by the intersection of (a) net-price-volume elasticity, (b) durability of clinical differentiation under PBM scrutiny, (c) launch cash-flow constraints, (d) long-term LOE positioning, (e) IRA exposure (a drug heading for negotiation in 8-10 years has different incentives than one that won't qualify). The classical mistake is pursuing Option 1 reflexively because "we need access" — without modeling that the floor-rebate scenario may produce a net-price profile under which the asset NPV is materially worse than Option 2 or 3, even at lower volume. Model all four explicitly with realistic volume forecasts and net-price-elasticity assumptions, then choose with eyes open. The PBM is going to model it that way regardless; the manufacturer that doesn't is negotiating from a less-informed position.

The river branches — retail, specialty, buy-and-bill, and the 340B side channel

+

A drug's channel is not a logistics decision; it is a commercial architecture. The choice between retail, specialty, and buy-and-bill — usually constrained but rarely fully determined by the molecule's pharmaceutical form — shapes the entire economic profile of the asset: who carries inventory, who bills which payer, who controls patient services, what data the manufacturer sees, what compliance regime applies, and what rebate structure is negotiable. Running parallel to all three, in shadow but at material scale, is the 340B program.

Day 3Learning objectives

  • Master the economics of retail, specialty pharmacy, buy-and-bill, and mail-order channels
  • Understand limited distribution networks and the data-rights implications
  • Internalize buy-and-bill economics — ASP+4.3%, GPO contracts, site-of-care dynamics
  • Grasp 340B mechanics — covered entities, contract pharmacies, the duplicate-discount problem
  • Understand 340B leakage and how manufacturers triangulate true 340B volume

The four key concepts

Retail vs. specialty pharmacy economics

Retail pharmacy dispenses most oral solid drugs through ~60,000 US locations under PBM-administered Rx benefit; manufacturer earns WAC less wholesaler discount (~2-4%), then nets out PBM rebates and copay program costs; pharmacy reimbursement on ingredient cost (often AWP-based) plus dispensing fee. Specialty pharmacy dispenses high-cost specialty oral and self-injectable drugs through limited networks (typically 5-10 SPs per drug), with mandatory clinical wraparound services (BV/BI, PA support, patient education, adherence monitoring). The Big Three SPs (Accredo, CVS Specialty, Optum Specialty) plus a layer of boutique SPs (Onco360, Biologics, AllianceRx, etc.). SP economics involve higher per-script administrative fees and richer data feedback (867 dispense, 852 inventory) — but also captive SP capture by PBM-integrated entities.

Two distinct riverbed types. Retail is the broad navigable river — high volume, standardized cargo, light-touch handling at scale. Specialty is the deep-water shipping channel — heavy cargo, specialized port handling, fewer ports, much more documentation and higher per-trip cost.

Buy-and-bill & ASP+ economics

Buy-and-bill applies to physician-administered drugs (most infusions, in-office injectables). The provider purchases the drug from a wholesaler at a price typically anchored to ASP (Average Sales Price), administers it, and bills the medical benefit. Medicare Part B reimburses at ASP+6% statutorily — eroded post-sequestration to ~ASP+4.3% practically. ASP is reported quarterly by manufacturers to CMS. GPOs (Group Purchasing Organizations) aggregate physician practice and HOPD purchasing — Onmark, ION, McKesson Plasma, IPD — and negotiate rebates that further reduce the provider's effective acquisition cost below ASP. Site of care matters: HOPD billing rates exceed physician-office rates substantially under Medicare, creating ongoing CMS pressure for site-neutrality.

A cargo channel where the dock-owner buys the cargo, stores it briefly, then sells to the end-customer at a regulated markup. The dock-owner's profit depends on the gap between purchase price (ASP) and reimbursement (ASP+%); when sequestration narrows that gap, dock economics shift toward lowest-cost-acquisition — increasingly favoring 340B-eligible docks and the larger GPOs.

340B mechanics & covered entities

The 340B Drug Pricing Program, established by the 1992 Veterans Health Care Act, requires manufacturers to provide steeply discounted prices on covered outpatient drugs to covered entities — disproportionate share hospitals (DSH), critical access hospitals (CAH), federally qualified health centers (FQHC), Ryan White HIV/AIDS clinics, and others. Discounts are calculated under a statutory formula, typically yielding a ceiling price ~50% or more below WAC. Originally intended for safety-net entities purchasing drugs for vulnerable populations, the program has expanded dramatically through (i) ACA 2010 DSH expansion, (ii) HRSA's 2010 contract pharmacy guidance, (iii) growth in DSH-eligible hospital systems. Annual 340B drug purchases now exceed $50 billion at WAC.

A statutorily-protected side waterway with steeply reduced tolls, originally created for emergency vessels (safety-net hospitals serving vulnerable populations) but progressively opened to more types of cargo and more types of vessels through expansive interpretation. The shadow channel now carries volume comparable to several of the main ones.

340B leakage & manufacturer triangulation

The central operational problem with 340B for manufacturers is volume leakage — the difficulty of identifying which dispensed units were actually 340B-eligible. A patient seen at a covered-entity hospital outpatient clinic may have their prescription filled at a contract pharmacy halfway across the city; the dispensing data does not flag the 340B linkage explicitly. Manufacturers triangulate true 340B volume through (i) HRSA's 340B OPAIS database of covered entities and contract pharmacies, (ii) chargeback data from wholesalers, (iii) claims-level analysis comparing dispense location to covered-entity affiliations, (iv) duplicate-discount analytics (Medicaid claims that should not also receive 340B). Recent manufacturer disputes (Eli Lilly, Sanofi, Novartis, AstraZeneca, etc., from 2020 onward) have restricted contract-pharmacy eligibility, prompting HRSA enforcement and ongoing federal litigation.

Auditing the contraband waterway. The water does flow through it, but who is using it, in what proportions, for which cargo, is partially visible at best. The auditor triangulates from upstream chargebacks, downstream dispense, and the registry of authorized vessels — and the audit always understates the true flow.

The price ladder — WAC, ASP, AMP, FCP, MFP, IRP corridor

+

A US drug has not one price but at least eight, each governing a different statutory rebate, regulatory benchmark, or commercial transaction. Confusing them is a routine source of internal modeling error and external negotiation failure. The discipline is to treat each price as a precise term-of-art with a specific statutory or contractual definition — and to know exactly which other prices it is anchored to.

Day 4Learning objectives

  • Master the eight US drug prices — WAC, ASP, AMP, BP, NADAC, FUL, FCP, MFP — and their definitions
  • Understand how each price is calculated, who calculates it, and what statutory rebate it governs
  • Internalize Medicaid best-price mechanics and why it is the silent governor on commercial pricing
  • Grasp international reference pricing (IRP) — corridor logic, US as reference source, ex-US sequencing
  • Build the basic launch price-setting framework

The four key concepts

WAC, AWP, & the published prices

WAC (Wholesale Acquisition Cost) is the manufacturer's published list price to wholesalers, before any discount or rebate. AWP (Average Wholesale Price) is a deprecated but still-used benchmark, conventionally WAC × 1.20, used by many state Medicaid and some commercial reimbursement formulas. NADAC (National Average Drug Acquisition Cost) is a CMS-published actual-acquisition-cost benchmark from pharmacy survey data, used by some state Medicaids. FUL (Federal Upper Limit) is a CMS ceiling for multi-source generic Medicaid reimbursement.

The published toll schedule on the lock. The number on the sign — WAC — is what gets quoted in public discourse and Bloomberg headlines. The actual rates paid through different combinations of contracts, benchmarks, and discounts are uniformly lower, and are what determine actual revenue.

ASP & the buy-and-bill benchmark

ASP (Average Sales Price) is calculated by the manufacturer quarterly as the volume-weighted average price across most US sales (with specified exclusions: Medicaid, 340B, FSS, manufacturer samples) net of most discounts and rebates. ASP is reported to CMS within 30 days of the end of each calendar quarter, calculated on a 6-month lag (so Q1 sales determine reimbursement starting in Q3). Medicare Part B reimburses at ASP+6% statutorily, eroded post-sequestration to ~ASP+4.3% practically. Inflation rebates under the IRA now apply when ASP grows faster than CPI-U, with payments owed back to Medicare.

The dynamic toll rate at the buy-and-bill lock. Recalculated quarterly from actual transactions, lagged so the current rate reflects what was paid two quarters ago, indexed against CPI-U so the manufacturer cannot raise the toll faster than inflation without paying a backflow tax.

AMP, Best Price & Medicaid rebates

AMP (Average Manufacturer Price) is the average price paid to the manufacturer by retail community pharmacies, calculated monthly and quarterly under Medicaid Drug Rebate Program rules. Best Price is the lowest price available from the manufacturer to any wholesaler, retailer, provider, HMO, or non-profit entity (with specified exclusions). The Medicaid rebate equals the greater of (i) AMP × 23.1% (innovator drug rate), or (ii) AMP minus Best Price — for innovator drugs. This Best Price floor is the silent governor of US commercial drug pricing: any price offered to a commercial counterparty below the manufacturer's current Best Price gets matched in Medicaid rebates across the entire Medicaid book, often making the discount uneconomic to offer commercially.

The "best toll offered to anyone" rule. If you charge any vessel a lower toll than your standard rate, that lower rate becomes the new ceiling for an entire category of vessels (Medicaid). The rule means generous one-off concessions can be ruinously expensive; commercial contracting is constrained at every step by the calculation of how the offer affects Best Price.

FCP, MFP, & international reference pricing

FCP (Federal Ceiling Price) caps drug prices for VA, DoD, IHS, and Coast Guard at a statutorily-defined formula — typically AMP × (1 − 24%) plus an inflation adjustment. MFP (Maximum Fair Price), introduced by the IRA, is the negotiated price for selected Medicare drugs, effective from January 1 of the second year after selection. MFPs apply only to Medicare beneficiaries' utilization and are reported separately in claims; they do not directly govern commercial pricing — though spillover effects through Best Price calculation and IRP are now central modeling questions. International reference pricing (IRP) — used by many EU member states, Japan, and others — sets domestic price by reference to a basket of comparator countries; the US, UK, Germany, France, Switzerland, and the Nordics are the most-referenced sources.

The federal maximum-water-level rule (FCP for federal direct purchasing, MFP for negotiated Medicare). And the international corridor — many ex-US countries now anchor their domestic toll to a basket of foreign tolls, with the US headwaters price as one of the most heavily-weighted reference points.

Mentor's note

The single most important fact about US drug pricing that experienced commercial leaders sometimes still get wrong is this: Best Price is not a concept; it is a calculation that runs on every contract you write. Every commercial discount you negotiate — every rebate, every administrative fee, every copay-support arrangement, every value-based contract structure — has to be evaluated not just for its direct cost but for what it does to the manufacturer's Best Price calculation, which then propagates through the Medicaid rebate formula across all Medicaid volume. A 1% incremental rebate on 5% of commercial volume, if it lowers Best Price, can cost the manufacturer 1% × 100% of Medicaid volume in additional rebates — a 20x amplification. This is why sophisticated contracting teams spend disproportionate effort engineering contract structures that achieve commercial objectives without triggering Best Price erosion — through bona fide service fees, qualifying outcome-contingent payments under CMS's multiple-best-price OBA guidance, exempted purchaser categories, and the careful structuring of upfront vs. back-end consideration. The legal and pricing teams that master this engineering save the manufacturer multiples of their compensation; the ones that don't generate Medicaid-rebate surprises that show up in the next quarter's GTN reforecast.

Building the value argument — economic models, AMCP dossier, RWE-for-access

+

A drug's clinical evidence answers the question "does it work?" A drug's market-access evidence answers the harder, more commercially decisive question: "is the value worth what we are charging for it, to whom, on what time horizon, with what budget impact, and against what alternative?" The value argument is built from economic models, real-world evidence, patient-experience evidence, and a coherent story that translates clinical benefit into payer-relevant currency. The work overlaps substantially with HEOR — but its purpose is operational: to win formulary decisions and price negotiations against specific counterparties, not to satisfy academic peer review.

  • Master the AMCP Format for Formulary Submissions (5th edition) — what payers actually want
  • Build cost-effectiveness and budget-impact models tailored to US payer archetypes
  • Understand which clinical and economic endpoints US payers actually weight
  • Internalize the role of RWE in pre- and post-launch access argument-building
  • Grasp how external value frameworks (ICER Institute, NCCN Evidence Blocks, ASCO Value Framework) intersect with payer decisions

The four key concepts

The AMCP Format dossier

The AMCP Format for Formulary Submissions, currently 5th edition, is the US standard structure for the value dossier that manufacturers submit to P&T (Pharmacy and Therapeutics) committees. Standard sections: (1) Product information & package insert, (2) Place in therapy with disease background and competitive landscape, (3) Clinical evidence from registration trials and supportive RWE, (4) Economic value with budget impact model and cost-effectiveness model, (5) Modeling report documenting input sources, methods, validation, (6) Supporting analyses (subgroup, sensitivity, alternative comparator), (7) Appendices (publications, study reports, model technical documentation). Most major US payers expect an AMCP-format dossier before P&T review, with country-specific value dossiers (CVDs) for ex-US submissions.

The full policy application packet submitted to the underwriter. Every diligence question pre-answered, every model documented, every alternative scenario explored, all under a standardized table of contents the underwriter can navigate without negotiation about format. The format is not a constraint; it is the shared protocol that makes the conversation tractable.

BIM & CEA for the US payer archetype

US payer modeling differs from HTA modeling. Budget impact models (BIMs) matter as much as or more than cost-effectiveness — payers operate on annual budgets, not lifetime QALY frames. Standard BIM horizon: 1–3 years; perspective: payer; key outputs: PMPM (per-member-per-month) impact, total budget over horizon, offset analysis (avoided costs from drug-attributable benefit). CEAs in the US payer context use payer-specific costing (commercial rates, Medicare Part B rates, Medicaid rates), often shorter time horizons than HTA-style submissions, and frequently anchor to pragmatic willingness-to-pay benchmarks ($50K/$100K/$150K thresholds published by the ICER Institute) rather than explicit thresholds set by the payer. Payer-specific economic models are typically delivered as Excel-based interactive tools the payer can re-run with their own population assumptions.

Two different stress-test reports for two different audiences. The HTA report is the regulatory stress test (lifetime, QALY-anchored, threshold-based). The US payer model is the corporate-budget stress test (annual, dollar-anchored, PMPM-output). The same underlying evidence base; different presentations for different decision contexts.

Endpoints US payers actually weight

US commercial payers consistently weight a tighter set of evidence than HTA bodies. (1) Total cost of care — drug cost plus avoided medical cost (hospitalizations, ER visits, complications). (2) Adherence-relevant outcomes — specialty drugs with strong adherence-effectiveness link command attention. (3) Comparative effectiveness vs. real-world standard of care — head-to-head when available, RWE comparison otherwise. (4) Subpopulation segmentation — payers want to know who benefits most so they can write UM criteria. (5) Budget impact predictability — narrow forecast confidence interval. (6) Implementation feasibility — can their UM systems actually operationalize the access criteria? Payers under-weight overall QALY arguments compared to NICE/IQWiG; they over-weight short-horizon BIM compared to lifetime CEA.

The underwriter's specific diligence checklist. Some questions are universal (does the policy reduce risk?); some are jurisdictional (does it fit our regulatory frame?); some are operational (can we actually implement the contract in our claims system?). The US payer's checklist tilts heavily toward operational and budget-implementation questions.

RWE-for-access & external value frameworks

RWE-for-access uses real-world data to support specific access arguments: comparative effectiveness vs. SoC where head-to-head trials don't exist, real-world adherence and persistence as predictors of effectiveness, total cost of care studies showing offset, subpopulation effectiveness for UM-criteria justification. External value frameworks — ICER Institute publishes value-framework assessments that increasingly drive commercial payer decisions; NCCN Evidence Blocks rate oncology drugs on five dimensions including affordability; ASCO Value Framework uses net health benefit and cost; DrugAbacus (Memorial Sloan Kettering) is a public CEA tool. These frameworks operate alongside formal AMCP submission and are increasingly cited in P&T deliberations.

Independent ratings agencies. Underwriters consult Moody's and S&P alongside their own analysis. Payers consult ICER and NCCN alongside the manufacturer's dossier. Bad ratings can sink the deal; good ratings can de-risk it. The manufacturer cannot ignore them; engaging proactively is a major strategic discipline.

Mentor's note

The most common mistake in US payer dossier strategy is to treat the dossier as a static document delivered once at launch, rather than as the opening position of a multi-year evidence dialogue with the payer community. The AMCP Format submission you make at launch is your strongest moment of evidence — pivotal trial readouts, freshest BIM, full attention from the P&T committee. But within 18-24 months, the payer wants to know how the drug is performing in their book — adherence, persistence, total cost of care, comparative outcomes against the SoC their book actually uses. If you have not committed to RWE generation for these specific questions at launch, you arrive at the renewal cycle with no fresh evidence, and the next contract is negotiated on budget-impact estimates that have been overtaken by reality. The dossier strategy that ages well is the one that says, at launch, "here is our value argument, here are the three RWE studies we are committing to deliver in the next 24 months, here are the questions those studies will answer, and here is the data infrastructure we are building to enable them." That posture — evidence as a continuing commitment, not a one-time deliverable — is what distinguishes manufacturers who hold formulary position over time from those who lose it at the first contract renewal.

The gross-to-net waterfall — where 50–70% of revenue evaporates

+

If you only learn one number-shape in market access, learn the gross-to-net waterfall — the cascade that takes a manufacturer's gross sales at WAC and reduces it, layer by layer, to the net revenue actually booked. For a typical branded retail drug, the waterfall consumes 50–70% of headwaters volume; for some classes (insulin, GLP-1, branded HIV) it can exceed 80%. The structure of that waterfall — which deductions are large, which are growing, which are negotiable, which are statutory — is the core diagnostic of the manufacturer's commercial situation.

Day 6Learning objectives

  • Master the gross-to-net waterfall — the standard layers and their typical magnitudes
  • Decompose GTN by channel — retail vs. specialty vs. buy-and-bill economics
  • Decompose GTN by payer segment — commercial vs. Medicare vs. Medicaid vs. 340B
  • Understand rebate-liability accrual — how manufacturers forecast and book GTN reserves
  • Internalize the dynamic GTN reforecast cycle and how policy changes propagate through it

The four key concepts

The standard GTN waterfall

From gross sales at WAC, the typical deduction layers are: (1) Wholesaler/distributor discounts (~2–5%), (2) Prompt-pay discounts (~1–2%), (3) Returns and chargebacks (~1–3%), (4) Commercial rebates to PBMs and insurers (~10–30% for branded retail), (5) Medicare Part D rebates and the manufacturer discount (~10–25% post-2025 redesign), (6) Medicaid rebates via MDRP (~15–25% effective on Medicaid book), (7) 340B chargebacks (depends on 340B share and ceiling-price-vs-WAC delta), (8) Patient services costs (copay programs, hub services, PAP), (9) Inflation rebates if applicable. Total GTN deduction for a typical branded retail drug: 50–70%; for some heavily-rebated classes: 80%+.

The gauge stations along the watershed. At each station, the engineer measures water flow before and after a known toll-collection point, computes the volume taken, and aggregates across stations to compute total losses from source to sea. GTN by channel

Channel materially shapes the GTN profile. Retail: large PBM rebate layer (often 30–50%), modest wholesaler discount, copay program cost, no 340B (most retail drugs aren't 340B-eligible volume drivers). Specialty pharmacy: rebate layers similar to retail but plus SP administrative fees and richer patient-services costs; data fees often netted against. Buy-and-bill: smaller rebate layer (because Part B reimburses at ASP+, the rebate-to-PBM channel is largely absent), but heavy GPO discounts, ASP+ erosion through sequester, and material 340B exposure. Mail-order: PBM-captured economics resemble retail at slightly different rebate rates.

The deduction profile differs by riverbank. Locks on the retail river charge tolls of one structure; locks on the specialty river charge tolls of another; the buy-and-bill river has fewer locks but a steeper 340B side-channel diversion. The total volume lost is similar; the structure of where it goes differs.

GTN by payer segment

Cross-cutting the channel decomposition is the payer decomposition. Commercial: PBM rebate, copay program cost. Medicare Part D: PBM rebate (commercial-equivalent for non-LIS), manufacturer discount in catastrophic phase (10/20% post-2025 redesign), inflation rebate when applicable. Medicaid: MDRP statutory rebate (greater of 23.1% AMP or AMP-BP), plus state supplemental rebates. Medicare Part B: ASP+4.3% reimbursement, Part B inflation rebate. 340B: ceiling-price chargeback (~50%+ off WAC). VA/DoD: FCP-driven discount. Cash: typically near-WAC realization but small volume. Quarterly GTN reforecasting requires modeling each payer-channel cell separately and aggregating.

The watershed cross-section. Volume flows through different channels at different proportions, and each channel-payer combination has its own toll structure. The same molecule sees radically different effective tolls depending on which channel × payer cell it flows through. Forecasting requires modeling the cells.

Rebate-liability accrual & dynamic reforecast

Manufacturers do not pay most rebates at the time of sale; they accrue a rebate liability reserve on the balance sheet for rebates expected to be paid (Medicaid 1–2 quarter lag; PBM commercial 1–2 quarter lag; Part D coverage gap discounts on similar timing; inflation rebates with annual settlement). The reserve is calculated through detailed channel-payer modeling that estimates expected ultimate rebate payments per unit sold. Quarterly true-ups reconcile actual rebate invoices against accrued reserves; material reserve adjustments flow through to reported revenue and routinely produce earnings surprises. The discipline of accurate GTN reserving is a quiet but high-stakes finance-and-pricing collaboration.

The actuarial reserve on an insurance firm's balance sheet for losses incurred but not yet reported. The reserve is an estimate based on historical patterns and current exposure; reality eventually reconciles against it; large reconciliation surprises move the stock price. GTN reserves work the same way.

The federal dam — the IRA deep dive

+

Every pharma strategy written before August 16, 2022 was built on the assumption that Medicare would not directly negotiate drug prices — a fifty-year bipartisan consensus that had survived the 1965 creation of Medicare, the 1990 Medicaid Drug Rebate Program, the 2003 Medicare Modernization Act's explicit non-interference clause, and the 2010 Affordable Care Act. The Inflation Reduction Act ended that consensus in a single piece of legislation, and the consequences are still propagating through every contract, every commercial model, every launch plan, and every LOE strategy in the industry. The IRA is the single most consequential piece of pharma policy in five decades, and understanding its specific mechanics — not the headline summary — is the difference between strategy that is current and strategy that is obsolete.

The four key concepts

The Drug Price Negotiation Program

Under the IRA, CMS selects high-spend Medicare drugs for direct negotiation. Eligibility: single-source brand drugs (no generic/biosimilar competition), ≥7 years since FDA approval for small molecules (≥11 for biologics), among the top Part D spend (initial cycles) or Part B spend (later cycles). Cadence: 10 Part D drugs in the first cycle (selected 2023, MFP effective 2026); 15 more for 2027; 15 more for 2028; 20 per year thereafter. Part B drugs enter from 2028. Selected manufacturers must engage in negotiation or face an excise tax up to 95% of US sales on the drug — economically equivalent to forced participation. Statutory ceilings on the negotiated MFP: 75% of non-FAMP for drugs 9–12 years post-approval, 65% for 12–16 years, 40% for 16+ years.

A federal dam commissioned by act of Congress. The watershed engineer cannot route around the dam; the dam exists, the maximum water level is set by formula, and the only question is how the upstream reservoir adapts. Refusing to engage with the dam attracts a 95% excise penalty — economically equivalent to having no asset at all.

MFP mechanics & propagation

The Maximum Fair Price applies only to Medicare beneficiaries' utilization — Part D claims for MFP-selected drugs adjudicate at MFP starting January 1 of the second year after selection. Commercial, Medicaid, 340B, and ex-US sales are nominally unaffected. But MFP propagates through three quiet channels. (1) Medicaid Best Price. CMS issued guidance in 2024 confirming that MFP transactions are excluded from Best Price calculations — preserving the existing Medicaid framework. (2) International reference pricing. Many ex-US countries reference US prices in their IRP baskets; MFPs are now public and citable, creating downward pressure on ex-US prices for the same molecule even though MFPs only nominally apply in the US. (3) Commercial reference behavior. Sophisticated commercial payers cite MFPs in negotiations as evidence of "achievable" net price, even where MFPs don't formally apply.

The dam's maximum water level is published. Other watersheds in other jurisdictions, that never came under this dam's authority, nonetheless reference the published level when deciding how high to set their own dams. The published number propagates outward through the network of references — far beyond the legal jurisdiction of the original federal authority.

Part D redesign 2025

The IRA restructured the entire Part D benefit, effective January 1, 2025. (1) $2,000 annual OOP cap for beneficiaries — the first true catastrophic protection in Part D history. (2) Coverage gap eliminated — the "donut hole" no longer exists; benefit phases are now deductible → initial coverage → catastrophic. (3) Manufacturer Discount Program replaces the prior Coverage Gap Discount Program: manufacturers pay 10% of negotiated price in initial coverage and 20% in catastrophic phase for applicable drugs (vs. the prior 70% in coverage gap only). The discount applies to a much larger volume base, fundamentally restructuring manufacturer Part D economics. (4) Plan-level changes: Part D plan liability shifts upward in catastrophic phase from 15% to 60%; reinsurance from CMS drops from 80% to 20%; LIS-eligible utilization treated separately.

The watershed has a new maximum-draft limit at the delta — patients can no longer be required to wade deeper than $2,000. The federal authority funds the protection partly by levying a new toll on the manufacturer (the 10/20% discount), partly by shifting more responsibility to plans, and partly by reducing federal reinsurance. Every actor in the system has a different cash-flow profile under the new rules.

Inflation rebates · the backflow tax

Manufacturers must pay rebates to Medicare when drug-price growth exceeds inflation, separately for Part B (effective Q1 2023) and Part D (effective Q4 2022). Mechanics: each quarter, CMS calculates the inflation-adjusted benchmark (the drug's launch-period price grown by CPI-U through the current period). If the drug's ASP (Part B) or AMP (Part D) exceeds that benchmark, the manufacturer owes Medicare the excess on every Medicare-utilization unit for that quarter. Rebates can be material — for drugs that have aggressive WAC growth histories, inflation rebates can exceed 5–10% of Medicare net revenue. Strategic implication: WAC-growth discipline is now non-optional for any drug with material Medicare utilization; the era of routine 5–10% annual price increases on entrenched products is structurally over for the Medicare book.

A backflow tax at the lock. If the manufacturer raises the toll faster than the published inflation rate, the manufacturer must pay the excess back to the federal authority on every passage through the lock by federally-covered cargo. The mechanism does not prevent the toll increase; it just claws the excess back. Manufacturers who price ahead of CPI-U are now writing checks to Medicare quarterly.

Mentor's note

The most important strategic adjustment the IRA forces — and the one that brand teams routinely under-weight — is the change in the asset lifecycle clock. Before the IRA, a successful brand could generate predictable returns from approval through patent expiration, with WAC growth and indication expansion as compounding tailwinds across the full exclusivity window. The strategic implications cascade. Indication expansion that adds Medicare-utilizing patients accelerates IRA exposure rather than diluting it. Pediatric or rare-disease label additions that were once pure upside now have to be weighed against their effect on the asset's IRA selection timing. WAC growth that would once have been routine now triggers inflation rebates and accelerates MFP exposure through Part B cumulative spending. Late-stage indication launches that were once high-margin extensions are now compressed against the MFP cliff. The brand teams that get this right are running explicit IRA-exposure scenarios on every strategic decision — not as a side analysis, but as the central modeling discipline. The brand teams that haven't internalized the new clock are still running pre-IRA strategy on assets whose economic profile has fundamentally changed. The gap between the two postures is widening every quarter.

The patient access funnel — where most launches lose 30–60% of expected volume

+

The space between a written prescription and a paid claim is where most drug launches quietly underperform their commercial forecast. The forecasting models describe a clean linear path — Rx written, claim adjudicated, drug dispensed, patient on therapy — and the operational reality is anything but. Patients enroll but never complete benefits verification. Benefits verification completes but PA submission stalls. PA approves but copay shock triggers abandonment. The drug dispenses but persistence collapses by week 12. Each station between Rx and persistent therapy has its own friction profile, its own intervention levers, and its own contribution to the ultimate "did the launch actually achieve forecast?" question. The market access professionals who win are the ones who treat the funnel as the unit of analysis — not the headline conversion rate.

The four key concepts

Hub services architecture

A hub is a centralized patient-services platform — typically operated by a third-party vendor (e.g., AssistRx, Lash Group, CareMetx, ConnectiveRx, TrialCard) under contract to the manufacturer — that handles the operational steps between prescription and dispensing for specialty drugs. Core functions: (1) Enrollment intake — patient registration, consent capture, demographic and insurance data. (2) Benefits Verification (BV) — confirming the patient's plan covers the drug, at what tier, with what UM. (3) Benefits Investigation (BI) — modeling expected patient OOP, identifying copay-program eligibility, flagging Medicare/Medicaid for compliance. (4) Prior Authorization support — assembling clinical justification, submitting on the prescriber's behalf, tracking approval. (5) Dispense routing — directing the prescription to an in-network SP based on plan and patient geography. (6) Refill management — adherence programs, side-effect counseling, persistence support.

The lock-tender service for patient-vessels. The lock itself is the payer's UM machinery; the lock-tender helps the vessel actually navigate it — assembling the right paperwork, confirming clearance, signaling when the gate is ready to open. Without the tender service, most patient-vessels are stranded at the first lock.

SP data flow — 867, 852, limited vs. open

Specialty pharmacy contracts produce two data streams the manufacturer relies on for visibility. 867 dispense data — patient-level (de-identified) dispense events including drug, dose, days supply, payer, copay, dispense date — typically daily or weekly. 852 inventory data — SP-level inventory snapshots showing stock on hand, weeks of supply, reorder cadence. Limited Distribution Networks (LDN) restrict dispensing to a defined set of SPs (typically 3–10) under contract with rich data-sharing and patient-services obligations; provides higher data fidelity at the cost of reduced patient choice and PBM-captive-SP friction. Open distribution allows any in-network SP to dispense; broader access but thinner data and weaker manufacturer leverage on patient services. Choice between LDN and open is a major market-access strategy decision at launch.

Two boat-tracking regimes. Limited distribution = a few certified docks reporting precise manifests in real time; open distribution = many docks reporting summary tonnage with delays. The first gives the operator a precise map of every cargo movement; the second gives broader access at the cost of operational visibility.

Copay programs & AFPs

Manufacturer copay programs reduce patient OOP for commercially-insured patients (excluded for Medicare/Medicaid by anti-kickback law). Patient pays $5–25 instead of true coinsurance; manufacturer reimburses pharmacy for the difference. Copay accumulators are PBM-deployed counter-strategies: copay program payments do not count toward the patient's deductible or OOP max — patient still bears full out-of-pocket exposure once manufacturer assistance ends mid-year. Copay maximizers spread manufacturer copay assistance evenly across the year to maximize manufacturer extraction. Alternative Funding Programs (AFPs) are third-party vendors that designate specialty drugs as "non-essential health benefits," excluding them from plan coverage, then route patients to manufacturer PAPs (designed for uninsured) — extracting manufacturer assistance dollars while shifting cost off the plan sponsor. AFP detection and counter-strategy is now a formal market-access discipline.

The lock-tender services that help the vessel pass through, met by counter-mechanisms from the lock-keeper that capture the value of those services for the lock-keeper rather than the vessel. The tender pays the toll; the lock-keeper structures the rules so the toll-paid status doesn't accumulate toward the vessel's annual passage limit. The vessel pays twice.

Abandonment analytics

First-fill abandonment — patient has approved Rx, walks away from pharmacy without picking up. Strongly OOP-correlated; published research shows abandonment rates climb steeply above $250 OOP and approach 70% at $1,000+. Time-to-therapy — days from Rx written to first dispense. Long tails (>30 days) typically indicate PA-resolution friction. Persistence × access decomposition — separating "patient stopped because the drug didn't work" from "patient stopped because access broke." A drug with strong clinical persistence at month 3 but failing at month 7 likely has refill-coverage break (PA renewal failure, plan year reset, accumulator exhaustion) rather than tolerability issue. The medical-grade flow analytics applied to commercial-grade revenue questions. Each station in the patient journey has a measured pass-through rate; the rate-limiting station is where the next operational dollar should be spent. The discipline is the same as supply-chain bottleneck identification — find the constraint, fix the constraint, the next constraint reveals itself.

Outcomes-based architecture — where contracts go to die without operational design

Outcomes-based agreements are easy to announce and difficult to execute. The standard launch press release describes a contract that pays based on real-world outcomes; the operational reality five years later is often that the contract was never actually triggered, the data flows for measurement were never built, the attribution methodology was never agreed, and both parties quietly let the contract lapse without the promised settlements ever being computed. The pattern is so common that "OBA as press release" has become a derogatory term in market access. The teams that build OBAs that actually work treat the operational design — outcome definition, measurement, attribution, reconciliation, dispute resolution — as the contract, not as an afterthought.

The four key concepts

OBA structural templates

Four primary OBA structures dominate practice. (1) Risk-share / refund-on-failure. Manufacturer refunds a defined portion of price if the patient does not achieve a clinical milestone (e.g., gene therapy: durability of response at year 2). (2) Milestone-based payment. Total price split into installments contingent on sequential milestones — payment 1 at infusion, payment 2 at 6-month response, payment 3 at year-2 durability. Common for one-time curative therapies with high upfront cost. (3) Indication-based pricing (IBP). Different price for different indications of the same drug, contingent on real-world use patterns; rare in US (limited by Best Price mechanics) but common ex-US. (4) Performance rebate. Standard pricing with retrospective rebate if population-level outcomes (adherence, hospitalizations avoided, A1c reduction) fall below threshold. Often the simplest to operationalize and the most common in chronic disease.

Four reinsurance contract structures, each appropriate to different insured-event profiles. Risk-share = catastrophic failure clause. Milestone = layered tranche payment. Indication-based = portfolio-segmented underwriting. Performance rebate = retrospective experience-rated adjustment. The choice is operational, not theological.

Data infrastructure

An OBA's enforceability depends entirely on the data flow that measures the contracted outcome. Key questions: (1) Outcome operationalization — can the contracted endpoint be computed reliably from accessible data? Hospitalization avoided is operationalizable from claims; "improvement in quality of life" typically is not. (2) Data source — claims (broad but thin), EHR (rich but fragmented), registry (curated but limited population), patient-reported (deep but operationally heavy). Many OBAs require linked claims-EHR or registry data that did not exist when the contract was signed and must be built during contract life. (3) Attribution — was the outcome attributable to the drug, or to confounding factors? Contracts often specify covariates, washout periods, and exclusion criteria that can themselves become disputes. (4) Cadence — quarterly vs. annual measurement, lag between event and reconciliation, retrospective adjustments. Operational data infrastructure is the gating constraint on OBA feasibility, not the contract drafting.

The reinsurance contract is unenforceable without the loss-experience reports that reconcile against it. If the loss-reporting infrastructure is not built before the policy is bound, the policy is decorative. OBAs without the data pipeline are policies without claims-reporting.

Best Price & CMS multiple-best-price guidance

OBA design has historically run into a fundamental Best Price problem: a refund triggered by failure-of-outcome looks, on its face, like a price concession that would lower Best Price and cascade through Medicaid rebates. CMS issued multiple-best-price OBA guidance (2020 final rule, effective 2022) that allows manufacturers to report multiple best prices for a single drug when prices vary based on outcomes-based arrangements meeting specified criteria — qualifying VBPs (value-based purchasing arrangements) can be reported separately from the standard Best Price calculation, breaking the multiplicative Medicaid cascade that previously made many OBAs uneconomic. Qualifying criteria: the arrangement must be a written agreement, must tie payment to evidence-based outcomes, must apply to specified populations, and must be reported through CMS-defined mechanisms. Strategic implication: the 2020 rule meaningfully expanded OBA economic feasibility for innovator drugs, but only for arrangements that qualify under the rule's specific criteria — a meaningful contracting-design discipline.

A regulatory carve-out that allows performance-based reinsurance contracts to be reported separately from the standard "best toll offered" calculation. Without the carve-out, performance contracts triggered cascade penalties that made them uneconomic; with it, they become a viable contracting tool — but only if the contract qualifies under the rule's specifications. Engineering the qualification is now part of OBA design.

Reconciliation & dispute resolution

The operational heart of an OBA is the reconciliation process — the structured, time-bounded mechanism by which both parties compute the contracted outcome, settle the implied payment adjustment, and resolve disagreements. Standard elements: (1) Measurement protocol — exact specification of the data, computation, time window. (2) Reconciliation cadence — quarterly, semi-annual, or annual computation cycles. (3) Computation party — manufacturer, payer, or independent third party (often preferred for credibility). (4) Dispute pathway — escalation procedure when parties disagree on the computation, including data audit rights and arbitration. (5) Settlement mechanics — how the rebate or refund flows operationally given Medicaid/Medicare reporting timing constraints. The reconciliation process is what makes the contract real; without it, the contract is a press release that lapses.

The arbitration clause and audit rights of a reinsurance contract. Pricing and coverage are negotiated upfront; the actual money changes hands quarters later, after measurement, reconciliation, and any disputes are resolved. The settlement infrastructure is what makes the original deal enforceable.

Mentor's note

The most important thing I have learned about OBAs in two decades of pharma access work is that the contracts that succeed and the contracts that fail are distinguishable in advance — and the discriminating signal is whether the operational team can answer five specific questions before the contract is signed. Three: exactly when do reconciliation cycles run, who computes them, and what are the timing constraints from Medicaid reporting requirements? Four: exactly what happens when the parties disagree about the number — is there an audit right, a third-party arbitrator, a defined escalation timeline? Five: exactly what is the operational lifecycle of this contract — how does it get renewed, terminated, amended as the asset's evidence base evolves over years? Contracts where the team can answer all five with specifics tend to operationalize and produce settlements that match the announced intent. Contracts where any of the five is hand-waved tend to produce settlements that never happen, or that happen years late and with disputes that consume more legal cost than they generate in economic value. One articulation

The dispute pattern is among the most common OBA failure modes in real practice. The resolution and the design lesson are intertwined. Diagnosing the current dispute. The disagreement is almost certainly about one or more of: (a) which patients are in the eligible cohort — adherence threshold, washout periods, exclusion criteria; (b) how durable response is measured — what counts as "response," what data source confirms it, what time window applies; (c) attribution — patients who discontinued for non-failure reasons (death from unrelated cause, loss to follow-up, adverse event unrelated to durability) — are they counted as failures or excluded? Each of these should have been pre-specified; the dispute reveals where pre-specification was inadequate. Resolution pathway. Step 1: invoke the contract's dispute-resolution clause. If well-designed, this triggers (a) data audit by both parties or independent third party, (b) escalation to named senior decision-makers, (c) defined arbitration window before litigation. If under-designed, the parties default to commercial negotiation under threat of litigation — slower, more expensive, more relationship-damaging. Step 2: the audit. Pull the original measurement-protocol specification, compare to actual measurement practice, identify specifically where the 27 disputed patients are anomalous. Three plausible findings: (i) protocol genuinely was violated by the measurement process — manufacturer must accept refund obligation; (ii) protocol was followed but the protocol itself was ambiguous in the disputed cases — both parties share responsibility, settlement is mid-range; (iii) protocol was followed correctly and payer is grasping for cost relief — manufacturer holds firm with documentation. Most real disputes are (ii). Step 3: settle or arbitrate. Most disputes settle commercially because litigation costs both sides relationship value and management time. The settlement number reflects the audit's findings on protocol vs. interpretation. Design lessons three years ago. Several specific improvements would have prevented or reduced the dispute. (1) Independent measurement entity. If a neutral third party (e.g., a registry sponsor, an independent CRO) had been contracted at OBA inception to compute durability, the result is hard to dispute on procedural grounds. Manufacturers and payers each computing their own version of "the truth" produces predictable disagreement. (2) Pre-specified edge-case handling. Patients lost to follow-up, patients who discontinued for AEs, patients with concomitant therapy changes — each needs a contractually-defined treatment, with both parties signing off in writing on a worked example. Real OBAs that work have appendices with worked examples for each edge case. (3) Annual interim reconciliations. Disputes that surface at year 5 of a 5-year contract are catastrophic; disputes that surface at year 2 are correctable. Annual interim measurements with non-binding indicative settlements force both parties to confront measurement issues early, when they can be corrected, rather than letting them accumulate. (4) Audit rights. Each party's right to audit the other's underlying data, with defined limits and confidentiality protections. Without audit rights, disputes degenerate into "trust me on the number." (5) Defined arbitration. A named arbitrator (or selection mechanism), defined timeline, and binding decision authority — to provide a credible default that drives commercial settlement. The strategic frame: the value of OBA design discipline shows up at year 3 or year 5, not at year 1. The brand teams that under-invest in design at signing pay for it in settlement years later. The teams that over-invest at signing produce contracts that operationally settle as intended. The cost of design is small; the cost of dispute is large; the asymmetry is severe.

The four key concepts

LOE & biosimilar contracting defense

When patent expiration approaches, the brand has a defined toolkit for managing the volume and net-price erosion. (1) Authorized generic (AG) timing. Launch an in-house AG before generic entry to capture share that would otherwise go to first-filers, often with rebate structures that protect the branded book. (2) Dual-brand strategies. Maintain the originator brand alongside an AG and biosimilar with distinct positioning — common for biologics where biosimilar uptake takes years. (3) Payer-segment plays. Different access strategies in commercial vs. Medicare vs. Medicaid — defend share where rebate-to-price arithmetic is favorable, accept share loss where it isn't. (4) Innovator life-cycle management. New formulations (long-acting, fixed-dose combinations, alternative routes), pediatric extensions, new indications — extending exclusivity windows on patentable improvements while the legacy molecule erodes. (5) Switching strategies. Patient-loyalty programs, copay-card extensions, prescriber-decision-support tools at the point of substitution. The right combination depends on the molecule, the class, and the commercial economics.

A water-treaty defense as a new tributary opens upstream. The watershed engineer cannot prevent the new tributary; they can negotiate which downstream basins draw from which source, time the flow rates to maintain primary-route volume, and engineer infrastructure that captures value from both flows simultaneously. The toolkit is well-developed; the discipline is selecting the combination that fits the watershed's specific economics.

Channel-shift modeling

Three structural channel-shifts have been reshaping pharmaceutical economics over the past decade. (1) Site-of-care migration in buy-and-bill. Volume has been shifting from independent physician offices to HOPDs (driven by hospital acquisitions of community oncology, dermatology, rheumatology), to home infusion (driven by COVID and ongoing site-neutrality debates), and back toward community sites (driven by CMS site-neutrality initiatives). Each shift changes the manufacturer's effective ASP, GPO mix, 340B exposure, and patient-services obligations. (2) IDN consolidation. Integrated Delivery Networks (Sutter, Kaiser, HCA, etc.) are consolidating purchasing and care delivery, often becoming both 340B-eligible and operationally sophisticated counterparties. (3) Specialty pharmacy captivity. PBM-owned SPs continue to capture more of their PBM book, narrowing the manufacturer's data and patient-services advantage of limited distribution. Modeling these shifts dynamically — rather than treating them as static — is now essential to accurate forecasting.

The watershed's channel cross-sections shift over decades. The river that today carries 60% of total flow may carry 35% in fifteen years; the side channels that today are minor become majority routes. The forecasting failure is to assume current cross-sections persist; the operational success is to model the trajectory.

IRA expansion scenarios

The IRA's first 10 drugs were the visible event; the structural effect is the cumulative cadence of selection over the next decade. (1) Part D coverage expansion. 10 drugs in cycle 1, 15 in cycle 2, 15 in cycle 3, then 20 per year permanently. By 2030, ~80 drugs negotiated, representing roughly half of high-spend Medicare drug categories. (2) Part B integration from 2028. Physician-administered drugs (oncology biologics, infusions, in-office injectables) enter the program — fundamentally changing the buy-and-bill economics for the Medicare book and propagating through ASP+ reimbursement structures. (3) Selection criteria evolution. Watch for statutory or regulatory adjustments to selection thresholds, eligibility windows, exclusivity definitions — each adjustment changes which assets are exposed when. (4) Cross-asset portfolio implications. A manufacturer with multiple high-spend Medicare assets faces compounding exposure; portfolio-level scenario modeling becomes the strategic discipline rather than asset-by-asset analysis.

The federal dam was commissioned with an initial release schedule and a permanent operating cadence. The early years are the ramp; the steady-state is the long-run cumulative impact. Strategy that focuses only on the immediate cycle misses the structural arithmetic; strategy that models the steady-state correctly anticipates the post-2028 environment in which most large pharma manufacturers will have multiple negotiated drugs simultaneously.

The M-V-C-X integrated operating model

The four pillars of US Market Access — Map (Payer & Policy), Value (Pricing & Economic), Coverage (Access & Hub), Contract (Channel & Trade) — are not parallel workstreams but a single feedback loop. Map identifies the payer landscape and policy environment, feeding into Value strategy (which prices and contracts are achievable). Value decisions cascade into Coverage mechanics (which formulary tier, which UM, which hub design). Coverage outcomes feed into Contract renewal decisions and channel optimization. Contract outcomes — net price, paid claims, channel mix — feed back into Map as updated counterparty intelligence for the next cycle. Done well, the four engines run continuously; weakness in any one undermines the others. The integrated defense in the post-IRA environment requires all four engines aligned against the cumulative threats.

A four-engine flywheel. Each turn feeds the next; the loop is the operating system, not the components. The integrated brand teams run all four engines on a coordinated cadence; the siloed teams run them as separate functions and lose ground at every handoff.

The jargon decoder

Three tiers · roughly fifty terms · the working vocabulary of the watershed.

Market Access has a vocabulary problem and an ambiguity problem layered on top of it. The vocabulary problem is that the field draws on regulatory, contractual, clinical, financial, and operational disciplines, each with its own term-of-art conventions. The ambiguity problem is that some terms — "rebate," "discount," "coverage," "access" — get used loosely in everyday brand conversation and rigorously in statutory or contractual contexts, and the difference between the two usages can produce expensive misunderstandings. The decoder below sorts the working vocabulary into three tiers: everyday terms used in casual brand conversation, technical terms with precise statutory or contractual meanings, and specialist terms that mostly appear in policy or legal contexts. Master the first tier, recognize the second, and know where to look up the third.

Tier 1 · Everyday

The vocabulary every brand-team member should know cold
WAC
Wholesale Acquisition Cost — the manufacturer's published list price to wholesalers, before any discount or rebate. The headline price. Almost no one actually pays it.
PBM
Pharmacy Benefit Manager — the entity that administers prescription drug benefits for plans, negotiates rebates with manufacturers, and runs the formulary. CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx are the Big Three.
Formulary
The structured list of drugs a plan will cover, organized by tier. Drugs on preferred tiers flow with low patient OOP and minimal friction; non-preferred drugs face higher OOP and UM constraints.
PA
Prior Authorization — the prescriber must submit clinical justification before the drug is dispensed; criteria vary by plan. The most common UM tool.
ST
Step Therapy — the patient must fail (or contraindicate) a designated cheaper drug before access. "Fail-first" therapy.
QL
Quantity Limit — capped daily, monthly, or annual dispensing of a drug. Often used to prevent off-label use or constrain expensive maintenance therapy.
Copay
The fixed dollar amount a patient pays per fill — typically $5–50 for generics, $25–100 for branded preferred, $50–250+ for non-preferred or specialty.
Coinsurance
The percentage a patient pays per fill — common on specialty tier (typically 20–40%). Produces high OOP for high-cost drugs and is the single largest driver of first-fill abandonment.
Rebate
The retrospective payment from a manufacturer to a PBM or plan in exchange for formulary placement and market-share performance. The economic backbone of the modern PBM business model.
AMCP
Academy of Managed Care Pharmacy — the professional society that publishes the "AMCP Format for Formulary Submissions," the US standard structure for the value dossier manufacturers submit to P&T committees.
Hub
The centralized patient-services platform — typically third-party-vendor-operated — that handles enrollment, BV/BI, PA support, dispense routing, and refill management for specialty drugs.
Specialty pharmacy
A pharmacy specializing in high-cost specialty oral and self-injectable drugs, with mandatory clinical wraparound services. Typically dispenses through limited distribution networks of 5–10 SPs per drug.
Abandonment
The patient walks away from an approved prescription without picking up. Strongly correlated with patient OOP. The primary leakage point in the patient access funnel.

Tier 2 · Technical

Statutory, contractual, and operational terms-of-art with precise meanings
ASP
Average Sales Price — the manufacturer's quarterly volume-weighted average net price (with statutory exclusions); the basis for Medicare Part B reimbursement at ASP+4.3% (post-sequestration).
AMP
Average Manufacturer Price — the average price paid to the manufacturer by retail community pharmacies; calculated under MDRP rules and used to compute Medicaid rebates.
Best Price
The lowest price available from the manufacturer to any non-excluded entity. The Medicaid rebate equals the greater of 23.1% AMP or AMP-Best Price for innovator drugs. The silent governor on commercial contracting.
NADAC
National Average Drug Acquisition Cost — CMS-published actual-acquisition-cost benchmark from pharmacy survey data; used by some state Medicaids.
FCP
Federal Ceiling Price — caps drug prices for VA, DoD, IHS, and Coast Guard at AMP × (1 − 24%) plus inflation adjustment.
MFP
Maximum Fair Price — the negotiated price for IRA-selected Medicare drugs, effective from January 1 of the second year after selection.
GTN
Gross-to-Net — the cascade of deductions from gross sales at WAC to net revenue booked. For branded retail typically consumes 50–70%; for some classes 80%+.
BIM
Budget Impact Model — the annual-horizon, PMPM-output economic model that US payers actually weight in formulary decisions.
CEA
Cost-Effectiveness Analysis — the lifetime-horizon, $/QALY-output model used in HTA submissions and cited contextually in US payer conversations.
OBA / VBA / MEA
Outcomes-Based Agreement / Value-Based Agreement / Managed Entry Agreement — interchangeable terms for contracts that tie payment to real-world outcomes.
IRP
International Reference Pricing — the practice of setting domestic price by reference to a basket of comparator countries' prices. The US is the most-referenced source globally.
DSH
Disproportionate Share Hospital — Medicare designation for hospitals serving low-income populations; one of the primary 340B-eligible entity categories.
FQHC
Federally Qualified Health Center — community health centers serving underserved populations; another 340B-eligible category.
MDRP
Medicaid Drug Rebate Program — the 1990 statutory framework requiring manufacturers to pay rebates on Medicaid-covered drugs. Created Medicaid Best Price.
MAC
Maximum Allowable Cost — the upper-limit reimbursement a PBM pays to a pharmacy for a given drug. MAC pricing is a major friction point in PBM-pharmacy economics.
P&T Committee
Pharmacy & Therapeutics Committee — the panel of clinicians and pharmacists at a plan or PBM that reviews drugs for formulary placement and UM criteria.
867 / 852
The two SP data feeds — 867 = patient-level (de-identified) dispense events; 852 = SP-level inventory snapshots. The manufacturer's primary visibility into specialty-drug volume.
BV / BI
Benefits Verification (does the plan cover it?) and Benefits Investigation (what will the patient actually pay?) — the two diligence steps performed by the hub before PA submission.
Accumulator
A PBM-deployed copay program counter-strategy: manufacturer copay support does not count toward the patient's deductible or OOP max — patient still bears full OOP exposure once manufacturer assistance ends.
Maximizer
A PBM strategy that spreads manufacturer copay assistance evenly across the year to maximize manufacturer extraction. Variant on the accumulator theme.
AFP
Alternative Funding Program — a third-party vendor that designates specialty drugs as "non-essential health benefits," excludes them from plan coverage, and routes patients to manufacturer PAPs intended for the uninsured.
GPO
Group Purchasing Organization — aggregates physician practice and HOPD purchasing for buy-and-bill drugs (Onmark, ION, McKesson Plasma, IPD). Negotiates rebates that further reduce provider effective acquisition cost below ASP.
IDN
Integrated Delivery Network — a hospital-physician system with consolidated purchasing and care delivery (Sutter, Kaiser, HCA). Increasingly sophisticated counterparties; often 340B-eligible.
PMPM
Per-Member-Per-Month — the standard unit of budget impact in US payer modeling. The currency of BIM conversations.

Replaces the prior Coverage Gap Discount Program effective 2025; manufacturers pay 10% of negotiated price in initial coverage and 20% in catastrophic phase for applicable drugs.
Inflation rebate
IRA-mandated rebate to Medicare when drug-price growth exceeds CPI-U; effective Q1 2023 for Part B and Q4 2022 for Part D. The "backflow tax" that ended routine annual price increases on Medicare-utilization drugs.
Multiple-Best-Price OBA guidance
CMS 2020 final rule (effective 2022) allowing manufacturers to report multiple best prices for a single drug when prices vary based on outcomes-based arrangements meeting specified criteria. The regulatory enabler for modern OBAs.
Contract pharmacy ruling
HRSA's 2010 guidance allowing 340B covered entities to use external contract pharmacies for dispensing — and the subsequent 2020+ manufacturer restrictions, ongoing federal litigation, and enforcement disputes.
OPAIS
The 340B Office of Pharmacy Affairs Information System — HRSA's database of registered covered entities and contract pharmacies. The starting point for any manufacturer 340B-volume triangulation.
Duplicate discount
The illegal scenario in which a Medicaid claim also receives a 340B discount — manufacturers cannot be required to pay both. Detection and dispute is a routine 340B-defense workstream.
Sunshine Act
The 2010 ACA provision (Open Payments) requiring industry to publicly report transfers of value to physicians and teaching hospitals. Database is public and searchable; relevant to commercial integrity and HCP-engagement strategy.
ERISA preemption
The legal doctrine that federal Employee Retirement Income Security Act preempts state regulation of self-funded employer health plans. Limits state-level PBM regulation and shapes state-versus-federal policy dynamics.
ICER Institute thresholds
The Institute for Clinical and Economic Review's published willingness-to-pay benchmarks — typically $50K, $100K, $150K per QALY — used as reference thresholds in US payer conversations even though no payer formally adopts them.
NCCN Evidence Blocks
National Comprehensive Cancer Network's structured rating of oncology drugs across five dimensions (efficacy, safety, evidence, consistency, affordability). Increasingly cited in P&T deliberations.
MAC-step
A combined Maximum Allowable Cost-plus-step-therapy UM design. Common in generic-and-branded multi-source therapy classes where PBMs constrain access to all but the lowest-cost option.
Fail-first
Synonym for step therapy — the patient must fail or contraindicate cheaper alternatives before access. Politically charged term often used in patient-advocacy criticism of UM.
Six patterns of quiet failure

The pitfalls that distinguish good market access from average.

Every functional discipline has a small set of failure modes that distinguish operationally excellent teams from average ones — patterns of error that show up repeatedly across organizations, are reliably recognized in retrospect, and are difficult to avoid prospectively without explicit anti-patterns in mind. The six pitfalls below represent the highest-frequency, highest-impact failure modes I have watched destroy commercial value across launches and in-line assets in the past two decades. None is sophisticated; all are common; each is preventable with disciplined practice.

01

Coverage-as-single-number reporting

The brand team reports "we have 80% commercial coverage" and treats it as a single metric. The number conflates lives covered, formulary tier, UM constraints, PA approval rate, time-to-fill, and first-fill abandonment — each of which has its own commercial implication. Most coverage problems are diagnosable only through the decomposed cascade.

Rescue: report coverage as a five-step funnel (Lives → Tier → UM → PA → Paid claim) every time. Treat the headline number as a starting point for diagnosis, never as a conclusion.
02

PBM as adversary or as friend, never as counterparty

The brand team treats the PBM either as the antagonist of drug-pricing-rhetoric or as a neutral administrator. Both postures are wrong. Adversarial postures produce contracts that fail at renewal; cooperative postures produce contracts that benefit the PBM at the manufacturer's expense.

Rescue: treat the PBM as a sophisticated B2B counterparty with structural information advantages. Model their incentives precisely. Engage on the merits with the same care a corporate-banking customer brings to JPMorgan — institutionally serious, not emotionally reactive.
03

Best Price erosion through poorly-engineered contracts

A 1% incremental rebate on 5% of commercial volume, if it lowers Best Price, can cost 1% × 100% of Medicaid volume in additional rebates — a 20× amplification. Brand teams that contract without explicit Best Price modeling routinely give away multiples of the headline rebate.

Rescue: every commercial concession runs through a Best Price calculation before signing. Engineer contract structures (bona fide service fees, qualifying VBPs under multiple-best-price guidance, exempted purchaser categories) that achieve commercial objectives without triggering the cascade.
04

Static GTN forecasting that misses 340B drift

The brand treats 340B as a static input to the GTN model. 340B share grows from 14% to 31% over 18 months. Net realization erodes 6–8 percentage points before anyone notices, and by then the contract-pharmacy footprint is institutionalized.

Rescue: dynamic 340B-share modeling with quarterly chargeback-vs-dispense triangulation. Pre-built playbooks for contract-pharmacy restriction, integrity programs, and HRSA engagement — executed before share crosses 25%, not after.
05

Hub data underutilization

Hub vendors produce rich funnel-decomposition data — patient-by-patient progression through enrollment, BV, BI, PA, dispense, fill-1, fill-2, persistence. Brand teams treat the data as ops reporting and never extract its strategic signal.

Rescue: weekly funnel-review cadence with the hub vendor as a strategic partner. Decompose by plan, prescriber tier, OOP band. Identify the largest-leakage station each week. Assign named owners with 7-day intervention commitments. Track the scorecard.
06

OBAs designed for press release

The launch announcement describes a contract that pays based on real-world outcomes. Five years later, the contract was never triggered, the data flows for measurement were never built, the attribution methodology was never agreed, and both parties quietly let it lapse without settlements ever computed.

Rescue: OBA design as systems engineering, not contract drafting. Specify all five operational layers — Outcome, Measurement, Attribution, Reconciliation, Dispute resolution — before signing. Independent measurement entity. Annual interim reconciliations. Defined arbitration.
Cross-domain connections

Three other industries have already solved these problems.

The structural-engineering challenges of US Market Access — managing flows through regulated networks, engineering transactions within statutory floors and ceilings, optimizing across multi-modal pathways — are not unique to pharma. Three adjacent industries have navigated similar challenges, often for longer than pharma has, and their solution architectures contain transferable patterns. The cross-pollination is rarely formalized in pharma training, but the analogies are precise enough to inform how to think about the watershed.

Cross-domain · Telecommunications

Interconnection regulation & universal service tolls

Telecom operators have managed regulated-network interconnection economics for over a century — negotiating access tariffs across competing carriers, navigating universal-service obligations, structuring revenue-sharing across the long-distance / local / wireless boundary. The architecture of "regulated common carrier with negotiated tolls at every interconnection point" maps almost directly onto the PBM-formulary-channel structure of US drug payment. Telecom learned that gaming the tariff structure produces short-term wins and long-term regulatory backlash; sustainable advantage comes from operational excellence within the structure. Same lesson applies to PBM relationships.

Cross-domain · Tax planning

Engineering legitimate structures within statutory floors and ceilings

Sophisticated tax planners do not minimize tax through aggressive interpretations; they engineer transaction structures that achieve commercial objectives within statutory rules — using bona fide business purposes, qualifying transaction categories, and explicit code-section safe harbors. The discipline maps directly onto Best Price engineering: every commercial concession must be structured to achieve the contracting objective without triggering Medicaid cascade. Treating Best Price the way good tax planners treat the tax code — as a set of explicit rules with engineering opportunities, not as a constraint to argue about — produces materially better outcomes.

Cross-domain · Logistics

Multi-modal routing optimization

Global logistics operators run continuous optimization across rail, ocean, road, and air modes — each with different cost, speed, capacity, and regulatory profiles. The decision architecture (which cargo through which mode, what handling at each transfer, how to engineer for resilience under disruption) is structurally identical to channel decisions in market access — retail vs. SP vs. buy-and-bill vs. mail, with limited-distribution vs. open-distribution choices, and 340B as a parallel side-channel. Logistics has a more mature optimization toolkit than pharma channel strategy currently uses; importing it is a meaningful opportunity.

The closing

Net access is what survives the watershed.

Ten days cannot make you a Market Access leader. The craft takes years — learning which payers respond to which arguments, which contract structures survive Best Price scrutiny, which channels protect net price under 340B drift, which OBAs operationally reconcile, and how the IRA federal dam reshapes every strategic question that came before it. The discipline is quiet, technical, consequential, and almost entirely invisible to the patient at the delta who simply needs the prescription filled. But ten days, done well, gives you the operating map. The map shows you where the locks are, who the lock-keepers are, what tolls each charges, where the side channels run, where the federal dam controls peak discharge, and where the volume evaporates along the route. With the map in mind, every commercial decision becomes a question about the watershed: what does this do to net realization, across which channels, against which counterparties, on what time horizon, under which regulatory regime?

Net access is what survives the watershed. Everything else is a number on a press release.

The watershed is more complicated than any human can fully model. It is also a known operating environment — every lock has documented rules, every toll has a published rate, every sluice gate has a position determined by upstream agreement. The market access professional's job is not to wish the watershed simpler. The job is to know it, navigate it, defend net realization through it, and engineer the flow that ultimately reaches patients. The watershed will keep changing — new dams will be commissioned, new tributaries will join, new tolls will be levied. The map is never finished. The discipline is to keep updating it.
